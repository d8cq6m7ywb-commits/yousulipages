Swim foundations.

From in-the-water to a comfortable 1,500 m freestyle

A free, structured freestyle course for adult athletes who can already get in a pool but aren’t comfortable past short stretches. Who this course is for

Honest, before you sink time into it.

Who this is for

You, if…

  • You can already get in the water without fear, but you stall out somewhere around 50 to 200 m of freestyle
  • You learned to swim as a kid but never had real technique coaching, or you started swimming as an adult
  • You’re training for a triathlon and the swim leg is the discipline you avoid
  • You can “survive” a 400 m but it’s ugly and exhausting, and you have no idea how to fix that
  • You want a structured way to get to 30 min / 1,500 m of relaxed continuous freestyle
Who this is not for

Probably not you, if…

  • You’re afraid of the water. That’s a different course and a different conversation. Start with a qualified swim instructor or sports therapist first. Come back when you can put your face in.
  • You already race comfortably over 1,500 m and want to drop time. This is too foundational. The race-prep work lives in the coaching path.
  • You expect to learn from text alone. Swim technique transfers through reps and feedback. This page gives you the structure, the drills, the tools, and a Slipstream-based way to get the reps. You still have to swim.

Where this course gets you

One clear milestone, written down so we both know what success looks like.

The milestone

A relaxed 30 minutes, or 1,500 m, of continuous freestyle. Not fast. Not pretty by Olympic standards. Just continuous, controlled, breathing properly, with a stroke that doesn’t fall apart at the back end.

If that sounds modest, it’s because it is, in a good way. 30 min / 1,500 m unlocks: most sprint and Olympic-distance triathlons, open-water swims at most parks and lakes, joining an adult swim club without anxiety, fitness swimming for the rest of your life. It’s the milestone after which the sport opens up.

Why distance, not speed

Speed comes from efficiency, not effort. Push hard on a broken stroke and you’ll be slower and more exhausted than someone half-fit with a clean stroke. We’re building the efficiency first. The pace will follow on its own, and it’ll keep improving for years after the course is over.

The 6-stage curriculum

Each stage has a clear objective. Don’t skip ahead. Each one assumes the previous is in place.

1
Objective · feel comfortable in horizontal hold

Body position and feel for the water

Before stroke, before kick, before anything: can your body lie flat in the water without your legs sinking? Most adult-onset swimmers are fighting gravity the whole time, which is why even short distances feel hard. We fix the float, the head alignment, and the basic awareness of where the water is pushing on your body.

The other big piece here is CO2 tolerance. Most beginners aren’t actually running out of oxygen, they’re running out of patience with rising CO2. Box breathing on land trains the same nervous-system response.

Drill: Sculling Drill: Side kicking Tool: Front-mount snorkel
2
Objective · breathing on a predictable rhythm

Breathing rhythm and the panic-free exhale

Breathing is the thing that separates a comfortable 1,500 m from a panicked 200 m. The single most common mistake: holding your breath underwater and then trying to exhale-and-inhale in the same brief moment your mouth clears the water. Doesn’t work. You end up gasping.

The fix: exhale continuously while your face is in the water, so when you turn to breathe, your lungs are empty and the inhale is automatic. We build to bilateral breathing (every 3 strokes) once the exhale is solid.

Drill: Pushing the wall Drill: Mixed breathing Tool: Front-mount snorkel
3
Objective · pull water, don’t push air

The catch and the pull

Where propulsion actually comes from. Most beginners pull with a dropped elbow, which means they’re pushing water sideways and down instead of backwards. We work on the high-elbow catch using a snorkel (so you’re not distracted by breathing) and paddles like the FINIS Freestyler (which punishes a dropped elbow by pulling sideways).

By the end of this stage you should feel the difference between a pull that sends you forward and a pull that tires your shoulders.

Drill: Snorkel double long dog Drill: Fist of fury Drill: Sculling (forward variant) Tool: Paddles (FINIS Freestyler)
4
Objective · kick for stability, not for propulsion

A kick that helps, not exhausts

For a 1,500 m at relaxed pace, your kick is a stabiliser, not a propeller. Adult-onset swimmers often overkick, which burns the legs and gets nothing in return. We work on a quiet, steady 2-beat or 4-beat kick from the hip, with relaxed ankles.

Ankle mobility is the silent limiter here. Most cyclists and runners have stiff ankles. We’ll work that in dry-land mobility too.

Drill: Unco Drill: Side kicking Tool: Short fins / Zoomers
5
Objective · swim continuously with a consistent stroke

Putting it together

Now we glue body position, breathing, catch and kick into a single repeatable stroke and start adding distance. The tempo trainer becomes your friend here: it sets a metronome for your stroke rate so you can find what feels right and stay consistent.

This is where the goal gets reachable: 5 x 200 m at conversational pace becomes 4 x 300, becomes 3 x 500, becomes one straight 1,500 m without a stop. The stroke stays the same. Only the distance changes.

Drill: Popov Drill: Swim band (advanced) Tool: Tempo trainer (FINIS Tempo Trainer Pro) Tool: Pull buoy (sparingly)
6
Objective · transfer from controlled to less-controlled

Beyond the pool (optional, but worth it)

If you want to swim in open water, race a triathlon, or join a masters squad, the last stage is about bridging from the pool to the rest of the swimming world. Open-water sighting, mass-start nerves, cold-shock acclimation, wetsuit basics. We don’t go deep here, that’s the next course, but we set up the bridge.

This stage is also where I’d nudge you toward a coached session or two if you haven’t already. Self-coaching takes you a long way; a second pair of eyes takes you further.

Tool: Wetsuit (if applicable) Tool: Pool buoy + tinted goggles

Tools that earn their place

You don’t need all of these. You need the right ones for the stage you’re on. None of these is required for the course; they accelerate it.

Tool 01 · stages 1–3

Front-mount snorkel

Removes the breathing variable so you can focus on body position, head alignment, and the catch. The single best piece of gear you can own at this stage. FINIS makes the standard one, with a smaller swimmer’s version for narrower faces.

Tool 02 · stage 3

Paddles (FINIS Freestyler)

The pivot shape forces a high-elbow catch and punishes a dropped elbow. You feel the error before any coach calls it. Better for technique than flat power paddles, which mostly build shoulder load.

Tool 03 · stage 4

Short fins / Zoomers

Speeds up the body in the water while improving kick mechanics. They promote good ankle mobility and kicking from the hips. Skip the long stiff fins, they distort the kick and aren’t a triathlon tool.

Tool 04 · stages 3–5

Pull buoy

Isolates the upper body and lets you feel rotation more clearly. Pairs especially well with the snorkel for technique work. Don’t make every set a buoy set, you do still need to remember how to swim with your legs.

Tool 05 · stage 5

Tempo trainer (FINIS Tempo Trainer Pro)

Cheap and surprisingly useful. Sets a beep at your target stroke rate so you can find your sustainable rhythm and hold it. Also works as a pacer (one beep per 25 m at goal pace) for the longer continuous sets later.

Tool 06 · ongoing

Goggles that actually fit

Two pairs you trust, one clear or low-light tint, one tinted. Replace them when the seal goes. A pair of leaky goggles fights you the entire session, no drill fixes that.

What it is

A portable swim machine

Slipstream is the first battery-powered, portable swimming machine: a turning-vane plus horizontal conditioning-grill setup that creates a wide, deep, even current in any pool. Drop it in a small backyard or hotel pool and you’ve got a stationary swim station. 1:20 / 100 yd top pace, 2.5+ miles per battery charge.

Why it changes the course

Drill reps without interruption

Stationary swimming means: no flip turns interrupting your rhythm, no lane traffic, no “wait, I forgot the cue” mid-length. A coach (or a camera) can move around you and watch the stroke from every angle. For a structured drill course like this one, that’s a force multiplier.

The eight things almost everyone gets wrong early

Reading the list is one thing. Catching yourself doing them is the work.

Holding your breath underwater

The biggest single cause of breathlessness in a beginner pool. The fix: exhale continuously through the nose while your face is in the water. Empty lungs at the rotation means automatic inhale.

Pulling with a dropped elbow

Forearm vertical, elbow high, that’s the catch. A dropped elbow pushes water sideways and tires the shoulder. The Freestyler paddle teaches this fast.

Looking forward, head too high

Your head is the rudder. Look at the bottom of the pool, not at the wall. A high head sinks the legs and triples the drag.

Crossing the centreline

Your hand enters in line with the same shoulder, not across the head. Crossing the centreline causes a wobble that you’ll feel as “snaky” swimming.

Overkicking

At foundational pace, your kick is a stabiliser. A loud, vigorous kick burns the legs for almost no propulsion gain. Quiet and steady, from the hip.

Trying to learn speed before efficiency

Going hard on a broken stroke locks the broken stroke in. Build the clean stroke first, even if it feels slow. The pace shows up later for free.

Tense neck and shoulders

You can’t rotate freely if your neck is locked. Drop the shoulders. Soften the jaw. Box breathing on land trains the relaxation response.

Treating drills as separate from swimming

A drill is not a warm-up filler. After each drill block, swim 50 to 100 m of full freestyle and try to keep the feeling the drill just gave you. That’s where the transfer happens.

A practice plan that actually works

2 to 3 sessions per week for 8 to 12 weeks. Each session 45 to 60 minutes. Below is the structure I’d use.

A typical session (45 to 60 min)

0–5 min
Dry-land prep: shoulder mobility, box breathing (5-5-5-5) for 2 to 3 minutes to engage the parasympathetic system, ankle circles.
5–15 min
Easy aerobic warm-up: 400 to 600 m of relaxed swimming. Mix freestyle and any stroke that feels good. Goal: get the heart rate up, not tired.
15–35 min
Drill block: pick 2 or 3 drills from the library that match the stage you’re on. 4 to 6 reps of each, with a 50 m of full freestyle after each block to transfer the feeling. Use the snorkel for most of it.
35–55 min
Main set: this is where the distance builds. Early in the course it might be 6 x 100 m with 20 s rest. Middle of the course: 4 x 200, then 3 x 300. Late in the course: 2 x 500 m, then 1 x 1,000 m, then 1 x 1,500 m. Tempo trainer set to a sustainable rate.
55–60 min
Cool down: 200 m easy. Roll the shoulders. Get out before you start thinking too hard.
8-week progression toward 1,500 m
  • Weeks 1–2: stage 1 + 2 drills. Main set: 6 x 100 m, 20 s rest.
  • Weeks 3–4: stage 3 drills introduced. Main set: 5 x 200 m, 30 s rest. Add bilateral breathing.
  • Weeks 5–6: stage 4 + 5 drills. Main set: 4 x 300 m, 30 s rest. Add tempo trainer at your sustainable rate.
  • Weeks 7–8: 2 x 500 m, then a continuous 1,000 m, then the one straight 1,500 m. Celebrate, then go again.
