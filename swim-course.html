Swim foundations.

From in-the-water to a comfortable 1,500 m freestyle

A free course I put together for adults who can already swim a bit, but stall out after 50 or 100 metres. The end goal is a relaxed 30 minutes, or 1,500 m, of continuous freestyle. The whole thing is built around the Slipstream portable swim machine, which I’ve found is the best single tool for working on freestyle in a small pool. Read it straight through if you have an hour, or jump to whichever stage matches where you are.

Who I had in mind

Worth checking before you sink an hour into reading.

Probably you

You, if any of this sounds familiar

  • You can get in the water without panicking, but you tap out somewhere around 50 to 200 metres of freestyle
  • You learned to swim as a kid and never had a coach correct anything, or you picked it up later as an adult
  • You signed up for a triathlon and the swim leg is the one you don’t want to think about
  • You can scrape through 400 m but it leaves you wrecked, and you have no idea which bit to work on first
Probably not you

Skip this one if…

  • You’re actually afraid of the water. That’s a different problem and a different course. Work with a qualified instructor or sports therapist first. Come back when putting your face in feels normal.
  • You already race 1,500 m comfortably and want to go faster. This is too basic for you. The work you need is on the coaching page.
  • You’re hoping to learn from reading alone. Swim doesn’t transfer that way. The page gives you a roadmap, drills, tool notes, and a Slipstream-based way to actually do the reps. The reps are still on you.

The end goal

One target, so we both know what we’re aiming at.

The target

A relaxed 30 minutes, or 1,500 m, of continuous freestyle. It doesn’t have to be fast, and it doesn’t need to look good. It just has to be sustainable, with breathing that works, and a stroke that doesn’t fall apart by the back end of the swim.

It’s a modest target on purpose. Once you’re comfortable there, a lot opens up: most sprint and Olympic triathlons, open-water swims at most lakes and parks, joining an adult swim club without your stomach in a knot, plain fitness swimming for the rest of your life. From that point on, you build instead of survive.

Why we work on distance before speed

Speed in swimming comes from efficiency more than from effort. If you push hard on a stroke that’s broken, you end up slower and more exhausted than someone half as fit who’s swimming cleanly. So we fix the stroke first. The pace shows up on its own once the mechanics are there, and it keeps improving for years after a course like this ends.

The six stages

Each one assumes the one above it is already in place. Resist the urge to skip ahead, even if a later stage sounds more interesting.

1
Goal · feel comfortable lying flat in the water

Body position and feel for the water

First thing, before any stroke work: can you actually float? A lot of adult swimmers I work with are fighting gravity the entire length, legs sinking, head pushing up, and that alone is why a short swim already feels hard. So we start with the float, the head position, and the simple awareness of where the water is pressing on you.

The other piece of stage 1 is CO2 tolerance. Most beginners aren’t running out of oxygen when they panic-stop, they’re running out of patience with rising CO2. A few minutes a day of box breathing on land (5 in, 5 hold, 5 out, 5 hold) trains the same nervous-system response we need in the water.

Drill: Sculling Drill: Side kicking Tool: Front-mount snorkel
2
Goal · predictable breathing rhythm

Breathing without panic

Breathing is what splits a comfortable 1,500 m from a panicky 200 m. The most common mistake I see is holding your breath while your face is in the water, then trying to exhale and inhale in the brief moment your mouth comes out. It doesn’t work, you end up half-empty and gasping.

The fix is to exhale continuously through the nose while your face is in the water. By the time you turn to breathe, your lungs are already empty and the inhale takes care of itself. We build up to bilateral breathing (every three strokes) once the exhale is dependable.

Drill: Pushing the wall Drill: Mixed breathing Tool: Front-mount snorkel
3
Goal · learn to pull water, not push air

The catch and pull

Now we talk about how you actually move forward. Most beginners pull with a dropped elbow, which means they’re shoving water down and sideways instead of pushing it backwards. We work on a high-elbow catch using a snorkel, so breathing isn’t a distraction, and paddles like the FINIS Freestyler, which yank sideways the second the elbow drops.

You’ll know stage 3 has clicked when a pull starts to send you forward instead of just tiring your shoulders. It feels almost lazy when it’s right.

Drill: Snorkel double long dog Drill: Fist of fury Drill: Sculling (forward variant) Tool: Paddles (FINIS Freestyler)
4
Goal · quiet kick that holds you steady

A kick you can live with for 30 minutes

At the pace we’re aiming for, the kick is mostly there to keep you balanced, not to push you forward. A common pattern with adult swimmers is to overkick, which burns the legs and barely changes the speed. What we want is a quiet 2-beat or 4-beat kick coming from the hip, with relaxed ankles.

Ankle mobility is the limiter most people don’t know they have. Cyclists and runners especially carry stiff ankles, so a little dry-land mobility work goes a long way here.

Drill: Unco Drill: Side kicking Tool: Short fins / Zoomers
5
Goal · one stroke, repeated, for longer and longer

Stringing it together

By stage 5, the body position, the breathing, the catch and the kick are all roughly in place. The job now is to keep them in place over more and more distance. The tempo trainer earns its keep here: a beep at your chosen stroke rate, and you just try to land on it, set after set.

The progression looks like 5 × 200 m turns into 4 × 300, then 3 × 500, then a straight 1,000, then a straight 1,500. The stroke shouldn’t change at all between any of those, only the distance does.

Drill: Popov Drill: Swim band (advanced) Tool: Tempo trainer (FINIS Tempo Trainer Pro) Tool: Pull buoy (sparingly)
6
Goal · carry the pool stroke into the rest of the swimming world

Out of the pool, if you want it

This one’s optional. If your end goal is just to swim 30 minutes for fitness, you can stop at stage 5. If you want to race a triathlon, swim open water, or join a masters squad, stage 6 is the bridge: sighting, mass-start nerves, cold-shock acclimation, basic wetsuit feel. I don’t go very deep here, that’s a course on its own, but we point you at the right things to practise.

This is also the point where I’d suggest getting a coached session or two if you haven’t already. Self-coaching gets you most of the way, but somebody watching can save you a lot of guessing.

The tools I actually use

You don’t need all of these, and none of them are mandatory. They speed things up at the right stage. The wrong tool at the wrong moment just gets in the way.

Tool 01 · stages 1–3

Front-mount snorkel

Takes breathing out of the equation so you can put all your attention on body position, head alignment, and the catch. If I had to pick one item to own, this would be it. FINIS makes the standard, plus a smaller swimmer’s version for narrower faces.

Tool 02 · stage 3

Paddles (FINIS Freestyler)

The pivot shape rewards a high-elbow catch and yanks sideways when the elbow drops. The paddle gives you the feedback before any coach does. Much better for technique than flat power paddles, which just pile load on the shoulders.

Tool 03 · stage 4

Short fins / Zoomers

They give you a bit of speed in the water and, more importantly, encourage ankle mobility and kicking from the hip. Avoid the long, stiff fins, they distort the kick and don’t carry over once you take them off.

Tool 04 · stages 3–5

Pull buoy

Floats the legs so you can focus on the upper body and rotation. It’s great paired with the snorkel for technique sets. Just don’t live in it, your legs still need to remember how to swim.

Tool 05 · stage 5

Tempo trainer (FINIS Tempo Trainer Pro)

Cheap, and more useful than it has any right to be. Set a beep at your target stroke rate and try to land on it. You can also use it as a pacer, one beep per 25 m, on the longer continuous sets later in the course.

Tool 06 · ongoing

Goggles that actually fit

Have two pairs you trust, one clear or low-light tint, one darker for sunny outdoor swims. Replace them once the seal starts going. Leaky goggles will ruin a session that no amount of technique work can fix.

Why Slipstream sits at the centre of this

The course assumes you can get access to a Slipstream swim station. Here’s why that’s the case.

What it is

A portable swim machine

Slipstream is a battery-powered, portable swimming machine. A turning vane plus a horizontal conditioning grill push out a wide, deep, even current in any pool. Drop it in a small backyard or hotel pool and you’ve got a proper swim station for the session. Top pace around 1:20 / 100 yd, and roughly 2.5 miles per battery charge.

Why I use it for this course

Drill reps that don’t get interrupted

Swimming in place means no flip turns breaking your rhythm, no other swimmers in your lane, and no losing the cue at the wall. A coach (or a camera on a tripod) can walk around you and watch the stroke from every angle. For a drill-heavy course like this one, that adds up to a lot more useful repetitions per hour.

Things I see beginners doing

Reading these and nodding is easy. Spotting yourself doing them mid-set is the real work.

Holding your breath

If I had to pick one habit that wrecks beginner swims, it’s this. Exhale through your nose the whole time your face is in the water. Empty lungs at the rotation means the inhale just happens.

Dropped elbow on the pull

The forearm should stay close to vertical, with a high elbow. When the elbow drops, you’re pushing water sideways and burning out your shoulder. The Freestyler paddle teaches this faster than any verbal cue.

Head too high, looking forward

Your head steers the rest of you. Look down at the bottom of the pool, not out at the wall. The moment you lift the head, your legs sink and your drag goes through the roof.

Hand crossing the centreline

The hand should land in line with the shoulder, not across your head. When it crosses, you swim in a soft zig-zag. You’ll feel it as a wobble more than see it.

Kicking too hard

At our target pace, the kick is mostly there to stabilise. Loud, splashy kicking burns the legs and barely changes the speed. Keep it quiet and let it come from the hip.

Pushing for speed before the stroke is clean

If you go hard on a broken stroke, you just lock the broken stroke in. Get the mechanics right at a slower pace first. Speed shows up on its own once the stroke is working.

Locked-up neck and shoulders

A tense neck stops you rotating, which is half the stroke. Soften the jaw, drop the shoulders, breathe. A few minutes of box breathing on land before a swim helps more than it sounds like it should.

Treating drills as a warm-up box to tick

A drill that isn’t followed by some normal swimming doesn’t transfer. After each drill block, do 50 or 100 m of regular freestyle and try to keep whatever feeling the drill just gave you. That’s where the change actually happens.

How to practise this

Two or three sessions a week for eight to twelve weeks, roughly 45 to 60 minutes a piece. Here’s the shape I’d give a session.

A typical session (45 to 60 min)

0–5 min
Dry-land prep. A bit of shoulder mobility, two or three minutes of box breathing (5 in, 5 hold, 5 out, 5 hold) to settle your nervous system, a few ankle circles.
5–15 min
Easy warm-up. 400 to 600 m at a relaxed pace. Mix freestyle with any other stroke that feels good. You want to be warm and breathing easily, not tired.
15–35 min
Drill block. Two or three drills from the library, the ones that match the stage you’re on. Four to six reps of each, with 50 m of normal freestyle after each one to lock in the feeling. The snorkel makes most of this easier.
35–55 min
Main set. This is where the distance grows. Early on, something like 6 × 100 m with 20 s rest. A few weeks in, 4 × 200, then 3 × 300. Toward the end, 2 × 500 m, then 1 × 1,000, then a straight 1,500. The tempo trainer keeps the rate honest.
55–60 min
Cool down. 200 m easy. Roll the shoulders. Get out, don’t hang around overanalysing.
An 8-week build toward 1,500 m
  • Weeks 1–2. Stage 1 and 2 drills. Main set around 6 × 100 m, 20 s rest. Comfortable, not heroic.
  • Weeks 3–4. Add stage 3 drills. Main set 5 × 200 m, 30 s rest. Introduce bilateral breathing on the easier sets.
  • Weeks 5–6. Add stage 4 and 5 drills. Main set 4 × 300 m, 30 s rest. Put the tempo trainer on a stroke rate you can hold.
  • Weeks 7–8. 2 × 500 m. Then a continuous 1,000. Then the straight 1,500 m. Allow yourself to be proud of it.
