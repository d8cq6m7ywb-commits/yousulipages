How to run a sweat test

Five-minute protocol — if you skip the bits in bold the numbers are noise.

01

Weigh in nude, post-bathroom

Empty bladder. Towel-dry sweat from the warm-up. Nude on a scale, recorded to 0.1 kg. Clothes hold sweat and skew the result.

02

Train at race-day intensity in race-day conditions

Same heat, same effort, same gear. Pre-weigh the bottles, post-weigh, measure exactly how much went in. 1 mL = 1 g for water-based drinks — just weigh.

03

Weigh out, again nude, again towel-dried

Right after stopping — before a shower, before a meal. Subtract bathroom breaks from duration if any (or just don’t go).

Run the test on at least 2–3 different sessions — one cool, one hot, one race-pace. Sweat rate doubles between cool and hot conditions, so a single number won’t cover race day if conditions change.

Are you a high-sodium sweater?

Six signs you lose more salt than the population average.

The 600 / 800 / 1200 mg/L numbers in the calculator bracket the realistic range — but where you sit on that range is genetic and can shift with heat acclimation. If multiple of these apply, anchor your race-day plan to the high column, not the average.

  • Visible salt residue on clothing, helmet straps, or skin after a hot session
  • Salty-tasting sweat — you can taste it on your lip without thinking about it
  • Sweat that stings the eyes noticeably more than other people’s
  • Strong salt cravings after long efforts — pretzels, broth, pickles, olives
  • Recurrent muscle cramps mid- or post-workout, especially in heat
  • Dizziness, low BP in heavy work; or feeling wrecked by hot races vs. cool ones

What % body weight lost actually means

The calculator tells you the number; this tells you whether to worry about it.

0–1%

Minimal

Within normal noise. Slight performance impairment possible; bloating if you over-drank.

1–3%

Normal

Most well-fueled training falls here. Thirst kicks in around 2%. Performance largely unaffected.

3–4%

High

Older studies show clear performance decline; recent data are more mixed. Either way, you under-drank for the conditions.

4–5%

Very high

Performance hit in most studies. Heat tolerance drops, cognition starts to slip. Rebuild fluid intake before the next session.

> 5%

Extreme

Significant performance and decision-making impairment. Real heat-illness risk — heat stroke, rhabdo. Stop, cool down, rehydrate slowly with sodium.

Pre · during · post hydration

What to drink, when, with how much sodium.

01

Pre-exercise (1–2 h before)

500–750 mL of a sodium-containing drink with 300–700 mg sodium per liter. Gives the body time to absorb fluid + salt before the session loads up the gut.

02

During exercise

Frequent small sips with sodium — promotes gastric emptying and helps carb absorption. Match intake to your measured sweat rate; don’t force-drink past 1 L/h unless your number says you need it.

03

Post-exercise (within 3 h)

Replace ~150% of fluid lost with sodium-containing drink (300–700 mg/L). Stimulates thirst, promotes retention, recovers electrolyte balance for the next session.

Reference: 1 tsp table salt ≈ 6 g ≈ 2 325 mg sodium (table salt is ~40% Na by weight). Salty foods that work: pretzels, pickles, olives, salted nuts, broth, vegetable juice.

Coach note · safety

Three populations should not race-day-test this number.

  • Older athletes — thirst response weakens with age. Don’t rely on it; drink to a schedule.
  • Athletes at altitude (~2 500 m+, non-acclimatized) — respiratory water loss adds on top of sweat loss.
  • Athletes with HBP, kidney conditions, pregnancy, or breastfeeding — consult a sports dietitian or your doctor before changing sodium load.
