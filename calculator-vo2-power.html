How it’s built

Five components turn VO2 into watts.

01

VO2max → metabolic rate

VO2 (L/min) = ml/kg/min × mass / 1000

Then × kJ/L oxygen (depends on RER) gives the kJ/min you’re burning at full VO2max.

02

RER substrate adjustment

kJ per liter of O2 depends on what you’re burning: ~19.6 kJ/L on pure fat (RER 0.70), ~21.1 kJ/L on pure carb (RER 1.00). Linearly interpolated. Race-pace cycling typically RER 0.92–0.98.

03

Fraction of VO2max

You can’t hold 100% of VO2max for long. The fraction drops with duration: ~95% at 5 min, ~85% at 20 min (FTP), ~80% at 60 min, ~72% at 3 hr. Auto-set by the duration dropdown.

04

VO2 on-kinetics

For durations ≤ 180 s, the mean VO2 over the effort is reduced by the time it takes O2 to ramp up. Mono-exponential, τ = 30 s. Matters most for 5K-track-pace efforts; negligible past ~3 min.

05

Gross efficiency → mechanical W

Cycling efficiency converts metabolic kJ into pedal-stroke work. Typical 18–24%, higher for trained cyclists. Calibrate from a known 20-min FTP to lock in your real value.

Coach note

Use this after a metabolic test, not before.

This calculator turns a measured VO2max into a power target. Three real uses:

  • Translate lab data into ride targets. You walked out with a VO2max number; this turns it into watts at FTP, race pace, Ironman pace.
  • Set targets for athletes who haven’t done a long race yet. A 90-min race-pace estimate from VO2max is the cleanest starting point before you have race data to fit to.
  • Sanity-check FTP outliers. If your CP test says one number and this says something 30 W different, dig — either VO2max measurement was off, or your CP test was paced badly.

Calibration matters most. The default 20% efficiency is a population average. Your efficiency might be 18 or 23 — that’s a 25% spread on output. If you have a verified FTP, run the calibrator first; the rest of the predictions come out much tighter.
