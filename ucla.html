What summer is actually for

If you only read one section, this is it.

Goal 01

Keep a small base & chip away at your biggest weaknesses

Two things at once: keep at least a small aerobic base so the fall calendar doesn’t crack you in September, and actively address one to three of your biggest weaknesses. Examples to be honest with yourself about: can’t fix a flat or hold a line on a bike? swim stroke that breaks down past 400 m? prone to a recurring run injury? Summer is a good window to address the things that don’t need too much in-person feedback to improve.

Goal 02

Lift heavy. Yes, really.

Off-season is when you build the strength + mobility that pay interest the rest of the year. Anatomical adaptation block, then hypertrophy. Not bodybuilding, just enough to make your tendons happy and your aerobic engine sit on a bigger chassis. Beyond efficiency, better conditioning reduces both how often injuries happen and how bad they get when they do. Nothing prevents every injury, but better conditioning nudges the odds in your favour.

Goal 03

Find your fueling baseline

Many endurance athletes under-eat. Summer is the time to fix that, when the stakes are low, not in October when they’re high. RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport) is what happens when you train hard on too few calories: hormones, bones, immunity, and performance all take a hit. It’s real, and the cost compounds.

I’ll remind you in every team session: carry hydration and nutrition to every training session, even the short ones. Fuel the work, treat yourself to the occasional croissant. Fueling like an athlete is not the same as dieting, and it definitely isn’t depriving yourself.

The path to Nationals: a working mental model

You don’t need to memorise this. But knowing roughly how qualifying works should shape what you do in June, July, and August. Read it once, then come back to it in the fall.

Working notes, some details below still being verified with the conference. I’ll update as I confirm specifics.

The team goal for next season

Do better at Nationals. Individual performances matter, but at Nationals the metric that ranks UCLA against every other club in the country is the club score. The goal is a balanced team, men, women, and the mixed relay, all able to do well overall on the day. Showing up healthy, prepared, and pacing a clean race is a team contribution even if you’re not a podium contender.

How I understand the club score is built at Nationals:

  • Men’s club score = top 4 UCLA men in the Olympic-distance race + top 1 UCLA man in the draft-legal race
  • Women’s club score = top 4 UCLA women in the Olympic-distance race + top 1 UCLA woman in the draft-legal race
  • Overall club score = men’s + women’s + mixed relay
The event itself

2027 USAT Collegiate Club Nationals (CCNC). Dates and host city TBA. The 2026 edition ran late March in Coastal Mississippi; expect a similar late-March / early-April window. I’ll update this section the moment USAT and CLASH publish the 2027 details. (Not-so-secretly hoping for Mt. SAC.)

Three formats typically run there: Olympic distance (1.5 km / 40 km / 10 km) split into a classic mass-start wave and a qualification-gated Elite wave; Draft-Legal Sprint (mass start, conference-allocated spots); and a Mixed Relay.

Three race pathways at Nationals

All three feed the club score. The Olympic Classic does most of the heavy lifting (8 of the scoring slots come from it). Draft-Legal contributes 2 scoring slots. The Elite wave is not a separate scoring category, it’s a faster start within the same Olympic race.

Path A · main team-scoring race

Olympic Classic

Open entry. No qualifying time, no ranking required. If you’re a current (or recently graduated) UCLA student verified by the registrar, you can race the Olympic Classic at Nationals.

This is the core team-scoring race: UCLA’s top 4 men and top 4 women in the Olympic-distance race count toward the club standings, that’s 8 of the 10 individual scoring slots.

Summer focus: build a body that can hold steady aerobic effort over 1.5 km / 40 km / 10 km. Volume + durability beat speed.

Path B · limited scoring spots

Draft-Legal Sprint + Mixed Relay

Both also count toward the club score, but with much narrower scoring slots:

  • Draft-Legal: only the top 1 UCLA man and top 1 UCLA woman score for the club, though up to 2 per gender (4 total) can race if the slots are earned. UCLA already has 2 of those 4 secured for 2026-27, one men’s and one women’s, as automatic bids from top-25 finishes in last year’s draft-legal race at Nationals (Clifton Thompson 19th, Josie Goodson 23rd).
  • Mixed Team Relay: one team relay score counts. UCLA’s allocation is TBD.

A slot belongs to the team, not to a specific athlete, so who races each one is a squad decision closer to the day. The other two DL slots (one per gender) are still up for grabs: earned by our top contenders through the season and conference rankings, or picked up in the roll-down after the regional races finish. MTR allocation is TBD.

Summer focus: be fall-race-ready by the home opener. The point-bank window opens early; athletes who train through summer have a real edge.

Path C · faster start, same race

Seeded / Elite Olympic Wave

This is not a separate scoring category. It’s the same Olympic-distance race as Path A, just a higher-level seeded start, cleaner racing, stronger field placement, and a better opportunity to place high. Standards are set at the conference level rather than published universally; likely some combination of ranking + license.

How WCCTC conference points work

Two different systems are at play, low-points-wins for raw race scoring, then a conversion to Omnium points where higher = better for the season-end team ranking. This is why a quick glance at wcctc.com/team-rankings shows the top team (typically Berkeley) at the highest point total, even though within a race lower is better.

Layer 1: raw race scoring (low = good)
  • Olympic race: points equal your place. 1st = 1, 2nd = 2, 10th = 10, and so on. A DNF or a missing athlete scores last place + 1, capped at 501.
  • DL race: points = (3 × place) − 2, so 1st = 1, 2nd = 4, 3rd = 7, 10th = 28. Only the first finisher from each club scores for the club; a second finisher displaces rivals but doesn’t add to their own club’s tally. A DL DNF scores last place + 1.
  • Mixed Relay: points = (5 × place) − 4, so 1st = 1, 2nd = 6, 3rd = 11, capped at 101. When two teams are close, the relay often decides it.
  • Raw team score: each team needs 4 athletes in the Olympic race. If fewer than 4 race, the missing spots each score last place + 1 (capped at 501). This keeps the scoring fair for clubs that show up with full rosters.
Layer 2: Omnium points + season ranking (high = good)
  • For each race, the team raw scores are ranked, and Omnium points are awarded: 1st team = 100 Omnium, 2nd = 90, 3rd = 80… 10th = 10. Outside the top 10 = 0.
  • Season-end ranking uses your best 2 or 3 conference races (2 if there are 5 or fewer races on the calendar including MTS, 3 if there are 6 or more) plus the conference championship (MTS), points doubled.
  • Same Omnium framework runs separately for men, women, and combined.
What WCCTC ranking actually decides

Per the official methodology: the in-season rankings do not affect team or individual entry into Nationals (CCNC).

What they do affect: your club’s Draft-Legal slots at Nationals. There are three ways to earn one:

  • Top 25 in last year’s CCNC draft-legal race (per gender) earns your club an automatic slot. This is how UCLA already holds 2 of its 4.
  • Conference championship: each conference gets 2 slots per gender, awarded on finishing place at the championship race.
  • Conference-size pool: 20 more slots are split across the conferences by size, awarded on each athlete’s best three conference-event finishes.

Unclaimed or maxed-out slots roll down to the next eligible club, so a strong season-end ranking can still pick one up.

Practical implication. You don’t need to grind every race. 2 to 3 strong races + a peak at MTS is a complete scoring season. If you want a DL slot at Nationals, you need a strong individual season-end ranking specifically, not just a strong team finish.

Eligibility fine print that shapes the fall
  • Race a conference event to qualify. To be eligible for Nationals, every athlete has to race at least one conference event in the 12 months before CCNC. A DNF still counts. This applies to para and able-bodied athletes alike, which is one more reason the fall races matter.
  • Full-time status matters for scoring. Part-time students can race the Olympic distance and win individual awards, but they cannot score for the team and cannot race the Draft-Legal or Mixed Relay events. So anyone we are counting on for team, DL, or relay points needs to be full-time, as defined by the registrar.

Weekly minimum standard

The weekly minimum: 1 swim + 1 bike + 1 run, weekly, all summer. Anything beyond that is bonus.

Why three sports, even when you’re tired

If you drop a discipline for 6+ weeks, the sport-specific neural patterns and tissue tolerance fade fast, especially the swim. You’ll spend the first 4 weeks of fall just rebuilding what you lost, instead of getting faster. One short session of each per week keeps the lights on.

Swim: 1× / week minimum

  • Pool or open water, 30–60 min
  • Technique first, then easy aerobic, then sprints
  • If one swim per week: 30 min with at least 50% done easy / drill

Bike: 1× / week minimum

  • One long aerobic ride OR a shorter ride with 5–20 min done at a somewhat uncomfortable intensity
  • 60–120 min is the sweet spot
  • Trainer counts. Group rides count more

Run: 1× / week minimum

  • Easy aerobic, 30–60 min, zone 2
  • Include warm-ups and strides (6 × 20 s) at the end
  • Trail / grass over pavement when possible

Strength: 2× / week ideal

  • Split the session upper vs lower + core, or do a mix
  • 30 min max, compound lifts
  • Details below in the strength section
⚠ Exception for fast-fall points hunters

If you’re targeting early-fall races for conference points (first 4 to 6 weeks of the season), the summer block needs more sport-specific volume, not less. Talk to me directly and I’ll build something custom for you on the Yousuli hub. The default weekly minimum doesn’t apply to you.

Sport-specific essentials

What matters most in each discipline this summer: technique, tools, injury prevention, and skills the team will be tested on.

Swim · technique > volume

Corrective drills + smart use of tools

Swimming punishes bad habits. 90% of the time-saving you’ll find in the pool this summer comes from cleaning up your stroke, not from swimming further. Slow yards done correctly build faster than fast yards done badly. Pick up to three drills per session that target your specific weaknesses, then do main sets in good form afterwards.

In swimming, don’t try to correct everything all at once. Three limiters to work on in a swim block is, for most people, the upper limit.

Tools that earn their place in your bag
  • Front-mount snorkel (FINIS, original or smaller swimmer’s version): removes the breathing variable so you can focus on body position, head alignment, and stroke mechanics. The best single piece of gear in the bag.
  • Paddles: my favourite is the FINIS Freestyler. The pivot shape forces a high-elbow catch and punishes a dropped elbow. The paddle gives you instant feedback. Better for technique than flat power paddles.
  • Short fins / Zoomers: speeds up the body in the water while actually improving kick mechanics, they promote good ankle mobility and kicking from the hips. Useful for body-position drills, vertical kicking, technique work at higher tempo. Skip the long stiff fins (not a triathlon tool).
  • Pull buoy: isolate the upper body and accentuate rotation. Pairs especially well with the snorkel, but don’t make every set a buoy set.
  • Tempo trainer (FINIS Tempo Trainer Pro): cheap and surprisingly useful for working on stroke rate and pacing. You can set a beep for every 25 y/m based on your target pace to make sure your sets are even.
Box breathing for CO2 tolerance

Most novice swimmers don’t actually run out of oxygen, they run out of tolerance for the rising CO2. The fix is training your nervous system to relax with higher CO2, not bigger lungs.

Box breathing. 5 seconds in, 5 hold, 5 out, 5 hold. Repeat for 3 to 5 minutes a day, on land. That’s it.

Two wins: it raises your CO2 tolerance so longer underwater phases / bilateral breathing / open-water chop stop feeling like emergencies, and it shifts you into a parasympathetic state (your “rest and digest” Buddha-like mode). Learning to forcefully engage your parasympathetic system is useful during races, before exams, or whenever your HRV drops.

Side note: I recommend the HRV Biofeedback app to monitor HRV in real time on your phone while doing guided breath work. Quick way to see whether your breathing pattern is actually doing what you want.

Know your APE index (wingspan vs height)

Measure your arm span fingertip-to-fingertip (arms out in a T) and compare it to your standing height. The ratio is your APE index. Most people sit right around 1.0. Elite swimmers often run 1.03 to 1.06 (long arms relative to height); shorter-armed athletes can be 0.97 or lower. Neither is a sentence: it just tells you what your stroke wants to look like.

  • Long-armed “Gorilla” (APE > 1.0): you naturally cover more water per stroke. Aim for a lower stroke rate, a longer glide, and a strong front-quadrant catch. Watch out for over-gliding (dead spot at the front), kick can be more of a stabiliser than a propeller.
  • Short-armed “T-Rex” (APE < 1.0): a longer glide costs you, you stall between strokes. Target a higher stroke rate, a more continuous catch, and a slightly stronger kick to maintain hip position and propulsion.
  • Body position is a separate variable: heavier legs sink faster, so a steadier 2-beat or 4-beat kick + good head alignment matters more for you. Lighter legs float, your kick is for rhythm and tempo, not lift.

Use the tempo trainer to find your sustainable stroke rate, not someone else’s. Track it in practice and on race day.

Curio: some pros have such good body positioning that, in a wetsuit, their legs ride too high. They actually modify their stroke or head position to make the lower body sink a bit so their kick becomes more effective in the suit.

Bike · skills + self-sufficiency

Handling skills & basic bike fixes

In collegiate racing, and especially in draft-legal racing, the time you lose to poor handling (sketchy descents, wide cornering, late braking, bad pack positioning) usually dwarfs the time you lose to a few watts of fitness. And the time you lose to not knowing how to fix a flat can DNF you. All of these can be picked up in an afternoon, but they need real practice afterwards to stick.

Note on drill mastery (true for all three sports): I only consider a drill mastered when it has been repeated hundreds of times and can still be done cleanly under stress and fatigue. Knowing the drill is step one; owning it is step ten.

Handling skills every roster member should drill
  • Cornering: outside foot down, weight outside, look through the corner. Slow speeds first, parking lot, cones.
  • Descending: relaxed grip, heels down, weight back on steep stuff. Practice on familiar roads in good conditions.
  • Group riding: hold a line, no surprise braking, signal hazards, rotate cleanly through a pace line.
  • Bike-to-run dismount: flying dismount (advanced) or controlled step-off, hit the line, don’t straddle in the chute.
  • Bottle pickup at aid stations, slow down, hand out, eye contact, don’t reach across.
  • Drafting rules: legal at most collegiate races (draft-legal format), illegal at most IM-branded races. Know the rules of the race you’re doing.
⚠ Basic mechanic skills (non-negotiable)

Before your first away race of the season, every roster member must be able to do these without help:

  • Change a tube / repair a tubeless flat with a plug
  • Re-seat a popped chain
  • Adjust front + rear derailleurs (basic limit screw + indexing)
  • Adjust / replace brake pads
  • Tighten / replace a cleat
  • Clean and lube the drivetrain (5-minute job, do it weekly)
  • Know your tire pressure, correct PSI for your weight + width (calc below)

If you can’t do all seven, I’ll run a Sunday workshop early in the fall. Bring your bike, tools, and a tube.

Run · durability first, speed second

Strength & drills for injury prevention

Running is the single most injury-prone of the three sports because of the impact load. Most collegiate run injuries (Achilles tendinopathy, ITB, runner’s knee, stress reactions, plantar fasciitis) are preventable. Not 100% (nothing is), but a good strength and drill base lowers your risk meaningfully.

There’s a second benefit that’s easy to overlook: when strength goes up, the severity of the injuries you do get goes down. The same fall, the same misstep, the same overload week, lands as a tweak instead of a tear, as a 1-week break instead of 6. Stronger tissues, stronger tendons, more robust connective tissue, and better neuromuscular control mean that when something does go wrong, you bounce back faster and lose less of the season. Strength isn’t just prevention, it’s also damage limitation.

Of the three sports, swimmers tend to have the best strength-training ethic and cyclists often the worst. The cycling problem is compounded by the fact that cycling is low-impact, which can reduce bone density over time if it’s not paired with weight-bearing activity (luckily for you, you run and you lift). I’ve seen many elite cyclists and swimmers take up running and get hurt for two reasons: (1) limited strength + sport-specific conditioning for impact, and (2) being elite means a strong cardiovascular system, which lets them push relatively hard paces, which makes strength + good form more important, not less.

The four strength priorities for runners
  • Core (transverse abs, obliques, deep stabilisers): keeps the pelvis steady so the legs can do their job. Planks, side planks, deadbugs, Pallof presses, ab wheel. Weak core = poor energy transfer across all three sports, unstable hips on the run, and lost stability with every breath in the swim.
  • Glutes (max + medius): the engine of run propulsion + lateral stability. Hip thrusts, single-leg bridges, lateral band walks, Bulgarian split squats. Weak glutes = reduced power transfer on the bike, knees collapsing inward while riding or running, higher knee-injury risk.
  • Calves & posterior chain (gastrocnemius, soleus, tibialis posterior): absorb impact, store/release elastic energy. Heavy slow calf raises (both straight + bent knee), heel drops, eccentric work. Most under-trained tissue group in this sport. Weak here drives most overuse running injuries, costs power transfer on bike and run, and kills your access to “free” running speed.
  • Single-leg work across all of the above, running is a single-leg sport. Bilateral lifts build raw strength; single-leg lifts transfer. Reality check: nobody is perfectly symmetrical. One leg is a touch longer, one quad slightly bigger, a tendon attaches a millimetre differently. That’s normal. Working one limb at a time screens for asymmetry and brings the weaker side up to a threshold that keeps you in one piece.

Run drills take ~10 min before every run (more if you’re sprinting). They promote good form, wake up the neuromuscular patterns that survive when fatigue hits late in races, and marginally lower injury risk by reinforcing good mechanics.

Reminder: dynamic movements before sessions. Static stretches only after sessions, they inhibit proper muscle activation if done pre-effort.

Big-stretch summer challenges (optional, fun)

Some of you are wired for big-effort single sessions. Here are a few that I’ve done myself or watched athletes finish. None of these are required; some will hurt for days. But if that’s what you’re after, here you go, they make great stories.

Read this first

Challenges are fun and they help with drive, but they are not the main work of summer. Your real priorities are skills development (see the swim, bike, run drills above) and building a strong, durable body. Pick one challenge as a stretch goal if you want, then go back to the boring consistent training that actually compounds.

Endurance challenges

The classics. Put aside a full day and bring snacks.

Swim

The Hero set: 100×100

100 reps of 100 metres or 100 yards, on a sensible interval. Most pools do this on 1:30 or 1:45. Total: 10 km if your pool is metric, about 9.14 km if it’s a 25-yard pool. A rite of passage for any serious swimmer. Plan it, fuel it, recruit a friend.

Smart version: negative-split the back half, mix in pull, paddles, and drills in the middle sets, sing the last 10.

Recommended internal soundtrack: “Watch Me (Whip / Nae Nae)”, “Baby Shark”, “I’m a Little Butterfly”.

Bike

Everesting

8,848 metres (29,029 ft) of climbing in a single ride. No sleep break. Pick a hill, climb it until you’ve summited Everest. Outside on a real climb, or indoors on Zwift / TrainerRoad / Wahoo SYSTM. Veloviewer verifies outdoor attempts.

For LA athletes: Mandeville Canyon gives roughly 315 m of climbing per rep. That works out to ~28 climbs of Mandeville to hit Everest (8,848 / 315 = 28.1). Plan 12 to 15 hours on the bike for most fit collegiate athletes, and don’t do this alone: mind the traffic, especially once fatigue settles in and your descending lines get sloppy. Bring a friend, a support driver, or both.

Run

The Zatopek session: 100×400

Named for Emil Zatopek, who reportedly trained on it. 100 reps of 400 m, separated either by a 200 m float (total close to 60 km of work, most of it running) or by a 90-second rest interval (total 40 km of work, mostly active recovery in between). Pick the format that matches your fitness. Use a track if you have one. Bring more gels than you think. And water. And lube. And toilet paper.

Smart version: first 30 reps at marathon pace, middle 40 at half-marathon pace, last 30 at marathon pace. Walk or jog the floats. Eat real food at rep 50.

Strength & control benchmarks

Less time per session, just as much character building. Each of these is a real test of strength-to-weight + control.

Pull-ups: 10–15 (men), 6–10 (women)

  • Strict, no kip, full hang, chin clearly over the bar
  • Builds the upper body that your swim catch needs
  • Realistic targets: 10 to 15 for men, 6 to 10 for women. Female athletes typically carry less upper-body muscle mass, so the bar is calibrated to make this a real challenge for everyone, not an impossible one for some
  • Most collegiate triathletes can’t do 5 on day one, let alone the upper end. That’s the point: it’s a benchmark to build toward, not a gate. Almost all can get there with consistent work
  • Way too hard right now? Start with Australian pull-ups (bar at hip height, body angled, feet on the ground), then progress to negatives, then half range, then full

35 single-leg calf raises

  • Standing on one foot, full range of motion, top and bottom
  • Each leg, 35 reps unbroken
  • Probably the single best Achilles / soleus durability test for a runner
  • If you can’t get to 25 right now, this is exactly why you might want it

Returning from a run injury? A line of research out of Stanford (and other groups) frames single-leg calf raise capacity as a useful readiness marker for getting back to running. The exact threshold varies by study, but the consistent thread is: low calf endurance + return to running = elevated re-injury risk. Build the calves first, then add the miles.

15 toes-to-bar

  • Hang from a bar, bring your toes to it, strict (no swing)
  • Crushes the deep core that holds your aero position
  • Use a partner / phone to verify your toes actually touch
Fuel for big efforts and recovery

If you’re going to attempt any of these, eat like you mean it. Endurance challenges burn a stupid number of calories; calisthenics challenges build muscle that needs protein to rebuild. Make sure your daily protein intake is high enough, especially across heavy training blocks. Rough rule for endurance athletes: 1.6 to 2.2 g of protein per kg of body weight per day, spread across 4 to 5 meals.

Plug your weight into the Yousuli Macro calculator for a recommended intake, then check it against what you’re actually eating for a few days.

Strength training: the off-season superpower

The single highest-ROI thing you’re probably not doing enough of.

Cyclists, runners, triathletes all have a habit of treating strength like a chore to “get back to later.” That’s how you end up with chronic patella tendon issues at 21, a stress reaction at 22, and a back that complains every time you sit in the aero position. Summer is when you fix that.

Phase 01 · June – mid-July

Anatomical adaptation (AA)

4–6 weeks of high-rep, low-load work (12–20 reps, 2–3 sets). Goal: wake up tendons, ligaments, stabilisers, connective tissue. Movements include split squats, single-leg RDLs, push-ups, rows, Pallof presses, deadbugs.

Why: tendons adapt 3–5× slower than muscle. Skipping AA = stress injuries when intensity ramps in August.

Phase 02 · mid-July – August

Hypertrophy & strength

4–6 weeks of moderate-load work (6–10 reps, 3–4 sets). Goal: actual muscle mass + maximal strength. Movements include back squats, deadlifts, bench press, pull-ups, single-leg work, hip thrusts, weighted core.

Yes, hypertrophy is the goal. The “I’ll get bulky” fear is for people who don’t run 25+ km/week. You won’t. Trust me.

Phase 03 · September onwards

In-season maintenance

Once racing starts: 1–2 short sessions/wk (20–30 min). Heavy but low volume, mostly to retain what you built. Stop dieting yourself out of the muscle you just gained.

Sets, reps, and the rule that matters most

Form > load. Always. If your knees cave in on a squat or your back rounds on a deadlift, the strength you build is the strength to hurt yourself. Film one set from the side per session, send it in the WhatsApp group, get feedback.

UCLA Wooden Center weight room hours, check the rec sports schedule. Group sessions on Tuesdays/Thursdays once school starts.

Fueling: eat enough, race ready

RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport) is the single biggest hidden problem in collegiate endurance athletes. Let’s not be that statistic.

What RED-S is · the 30-second version

When your training energy expenditure consistently exceeds your intake, your body shuts down “optional” systems: hormones drop, immune function dips, periods stop (women), libido / morning erections vanish (men), bone density falls. You feel slow, you keep getting sick, performance plateaus and then drops. It can take years to unwind. Easier to prevent than fix.

Eat breakfast within 60 min of waking

  • Carbs + protein + a little fat
  • Skipping breakfast + training = RED-S fast lane
  • Oats + Greek yogurt + banana = 90 seconds

Refuel within 60 min after sessions

  • ~30 g carbs + ~20 g protein minimum
  • Chocolate milk, recovery shake, rice + chicken, PB&J
  • Not optional, even after “easy” days

Fuel during sessions > 75 min

  • 30–60 g carbs/hr on bike + long runs
  • Gels, Maurten, Neversecond C30, real food
  • Train your gut to tolerate intake, it’s a skill

Don’t under-eat on rest days

  • RMR keeps running. Recovery needs fuel
  • Calorie cycling is fine; calorie crashing is not
  • Rule of thumb: never below body weight (kg) × 30 kcal
⚠ Signs you might be under-fueling
  • Resting HR creeping up week-over-week
  • Constant low-grade fatigue / disturbed sleep
  • Frequent colds, slow recovery from minor injuries
  • Loss of menstrual cycle (women) / loss of morning erections, libido (men)
  • Stalled or declining performance despite more training

If three of these are happening: stop, talk to me, see a sports physician. I can run a screening test that might shed light on the state of your metabolism.

Reading: six books that won’t hurt to have on your shelf

I read health and fitness content most days and roughly a book a week. These are not textbooks or the science books. These are six picks that are either fun, thought-provoking, or just useful for widening the lens. No particular order, none of them required, all easy to get through.

01 · Female physiology, evidence + practice

ROAR
Stacy T. Sims

The book most female endurance athletes wish they’d read at 18. Physiology, fueling, recovery, hormone-cycle considerations, all framed for women, not men’s data badly adapted. Worth a read for everyone on the roster, not just female athletes, the parts on fueling and recovery apply universally.

02 · Wide-the-view fitness

How To Make Yourself Poop
Runner’s World

Yes, that’s the title. A surprisingly useful book of small practical fixes any endurance athlete will recognise as “wait, I’ve had that problem.” Light reading, high hit rate.

03 · People & communication

Surrounded by Idiots
Thomas Erikson

Has nothing to do with training and that’s the point. A simple four-colour personality framework that’ll change how you talk to teammates, coaches, professors, and (eventually) bosses. Tri teams are a social experiment as much as an athletic one, useful tool for navigating it.

04 · Fueling & food culture

Diet Cult
Matt Fitzgerald

Why every “one true diet” ends up looking like a religion, and why the actual evidence says the boring thing: eat mostly whole foods, enough calories, vary the sources. Useful armor against TikTok nutrition advice and the worse end of athlete dieting culture.

05 · The mental side of the sport

How Bad Do You Want It?
Matt Fitzgerald

Brain-bound performance limits, told through real race stories. The chapters on perceived effort and pacing under fatigue are particularly useful if you race a lot of sprint and Olympic where the “hurt” window is the whole event.

06 · Memoir & the system

Good For a Girl
Lauren Fleshman

Honest memoir from inside the US distance-running system, including the parts the system would rather you didn’t see (under-fueling, body image, the way elite female athletes have historically been coached). Hard to put down, and harder to forget once you have.

If one of these clicks and you want the deeper-cut science companion afterwards, ask me, I have a long list for every one of them.

Team Q&A

Stuff that’ll come up. Email me anything not covered.

I’m brand-new to one of the three sports. Help.

Great. Summer is the time to be bad at things. Pick the weakest sport, do two short sessions/week minimum, focus 80% on technique. You’ll be embarrassingly improved by September. Promise.

I have an injury / niggle. What now?

Tell me before you train through it for three weeks and turn a niggle into surgery. I’ll modify the plan, swap sessions, get you to a PT if needed. Most niggles get fixed in a week of smart loading, if you act early.

How much sleep should I actually get?

Target 8 hours. Below 7 you’re training for resistance, not adaptation. Sleep is the single most powerful legal PED. Treat it that way.

Can I race in the summer?

Yes. A low-key Olympic or 70.3 in July or August is a great fitness check. Tell me 4 to 6 weeks out so I can plan the taper with you. Don’t sign up for a full Ironman on two weeks’ notice, especially if you’re prone to foot injury. (You know who you are.) The full-distance jump from where most of you are right now is too much body too fast.

Anything I’ll actually need to spend on?

Coaching, scheduling, the hub, summer guidance, the calculators, the 20-levers course, race-day support: all part of the roster. Race-day fuel products and any non-team races you sign up for are on you, like anywhere. Don’t feel pressure to buy gear or supplements you don’t need. If you ever want to dig deeper with lab testing or anything beyond the team setup, that’s a separate conversation, just ask me.

Triathlon can be affordable, and it can also be as expensive as you want it to be. A bling bike may get you clout, but it won’t necessarily make you faster. Ask Diderot about his jacket.

What if my goal is just to have fun and finish?

Perfect. The plan still applies, just at lower volume. Triathlon is for everyone; performance is a spectrum. Show up, train consistently, finish your races. That’s already 90th percentile of human beings.
