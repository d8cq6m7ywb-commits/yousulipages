What summer is actually for

If you only read one section, this is it.

Goal 01

Build a base that doesn’t break

The fall race calendar lands on you fast. Show up in September with a body that’s ready to absorb hard training, not one that’s already cracked from a spring of cramming. Volume here is moderate, consistency is everything.

Goal 02

Lift heavy. Yes, really.

Off-season is when you build the strength that pays interest the rest of the year. Anatomical adaptation block, then hypertrophy. Not bodybuilding, just enough to make your tendons happy and your aerobic engine sit on a bigger chassis.

Goal 03

Find your fueling baseline

College athletes under-eat. Summer is the time to fix that, when the stakes are low, not in October when they’re high. RED-S is real, and the cost compounds.

Even in the offseason: when you train, carry a bottle, hydrate, fuel the work. And treat yourself to a croissant once in a while. Fueling like an athlete isn’t the same as dieting, and it definitely isn’t depriving yourself.

How conference points work

Short version: it’s a low-points-wins system. Three of your best races + the championship (points doubled) decide your rank.

The scoring mechanic
  • Per-race points: 1st = 1 pt, 2nd = 2 pt… (DL races double-step: 1st = 1, 2nd = 3, 3rd = 5…). Cap at 100.
  • Season scoring: best 3 races + the championship race (doubled). 8 scoring races on the 2025–26 calendar.
  • Lower total = higher rank. Tie-breaker: lower points at the championship.
  • Practical implication. You don’t need to grind every race. 3 strong early races + a peak at the championship is a complete scoring season.

Weekly minimum standard

The non-negotiable: 1 swim + 1 bike + 1 run, every week, all summer. Anything beyond that is bonus.

Why three sports, even when you’re tired

If you drop a discipline for 6+ weeks, the sport-specific neural patterns and tissue tolerance fade fast, especially the swim. You’ll spend the first 4 weeks of fall just rebuilding what you lost, instead of getting faster. One short session of each per week keeps the lights on.

Swim: 1× / week minimum

  • Pool or open water, 30–60 min
  • Technique-first. Drills + steady aerobic
  • If only 1 swim/wk: keep it short & clean (3 × 200 m smooth + drills)

Bike: 1× / week minimum

  • One long aerobic ride OR an FTP-zone session
  • 60–120 min is the sweet spot
  • Trainer counts. Group rides count more

Run: 1× / week minimum

  • Easy aerobic, 30–60 min, zone 2
  • Add strides (6 × 20 s) at the end
  • Trail / grass over pavement when possible

Strength: 2× / week ideal

  • One full-body lower, one upper-pull / core
  • 30–45 min, compound lifts
  • Details below in the strength section
⚠ Exception for fast-fall points hunters

If you’re targeting early-fall races for conference points (first 4 to 6 weeks of the season), the summer block needs more sport-specific volume, not less. Talk to me directly and I’ll build something custom for you in TrainingPeaks. The default weekly minimum doesn’t apply to you.

Sport-specific essentials

What matters most in each discipline this summer: technique, tools, injury prevention, and skills the team will be tested on.

Swim · technique > volume

Corrective drills + smart use of tools

Swimming punishes bad habits. 90% of the time-saving you’ll find in the pool this summer comes from cleaning up your stroke, not from swimming further. Slow yards done correctly build faster than fast yards done badly. Pick one drill per session that targets your specific weakness, then do main sets in good form afterwards.

Tools that earn their place in your bag
  • Front-mount snorkel (FINIS, original or smaller swimmer’s version): removes the breathing variable so you can focus on body position, head alignment, and stroke mechanics. The best single piece of gear in the bag.
  • Paddles: my favourite is the FINIS Freestyler. The pivot shape forces a high-elbow catch and punishes a dropped elbow. You feel the error before the coach calls it. Better for technique than flat power paddles.
  • Short fins / Zoomers: speeds up the body in the water without distorting kick mechanics. Useful for body-position drills, vertical kicking, technique work at higher tempo. Skip the long stiff fins (not a triathlon tool).
  • Pull buoy: isolate the upper body, fix the rotation. Use sparingly, don’t make every set a buoy set.
  • Tempo trainer (FINIS Tempo Trainer Pro): cheap, life-changing. Trains stroke rate. You’ll do specific sets with it once school starts.

The drill rotation you’ll cycle through: catch-up, single-arm, fingertip drag, 6-3-6, zipper drill, fist drill, sculling. Each one targets a specific failure mode. Videos below show form.

Bike · skills + self-sufficiency

Handling skills & basic bike fixes

In collegiate racing, the time you lose to poor handling (sketchy descents, wide cornering, late braking, bad pack positioning) usually dwarfs the time you lose to a few watts of fitness. And the time you lose to not knowing how to fix a flat can DNF you. Both are learnable in an afternoon.

Handling skills every roster member should drill
  • Cornering: outside foot down, weight outside, look through the corner. Slow speeds first, parking lot, cones.
  • Descending: relaxed grip, heels down, weight back on steep stuff. Practice on familiar roads in good conditions.
  • Group riding: hold a line, no surprise braking, signal hazards, rotate cleanly through a pace line.
  • Bike-to-run dismount: flying dismount (advanced) or controlled step-off, hit the line, don’t straddle in the chute.
  • Bottle pickup at aid stations, slow down, hand out, eye contact, don’t reach across.
  • Drafting rules: legal at most collegiate races (draft-legal format), illegal at most IM-branded races. Know the rules of the race you’re doing.
⚠ Basic mechanic skills (non-negotiable)

Before your first away race of the season, every roster member must be able to do these without help:

  • Change a tube / repair a tubeless flat with a plug
  • Re-seat a popped chain
  • Adjust front + rear derailleurs (basic limit screw + indexing)
  • Adjust / replace brake pads
  • Tighten / replace a cleat
  • Clean and lube the drivetrain (5-minute job, do it weekly)
  • Know your tire pressure, correct PSI for your weight + width (calc below)

If you can’t do all seven, I’ll run a Sunday workshop early in the fall. Bring your bike, tools, and a tube.

Run · durability first, speed second

Strength & drills for injury prevention

Running is the single most injury-prone of the three sports because of the impact load. Most collegiate run injuries (Achilles tendinopathy, ITB, runner’s knee, stress reactions, plantar fasciitis) are preventable. Not 100% (nothing is), but a good strength and drill base lowers your risk meaningfully.

The four strength priorities for runners
  • Core (transverse abs, obliques, deep stabilisers): keeps the pelvis steady so the legs can do their job. Planks, side planks, deadbugs, Pallof presses, ab wheel.
  • Glutes (max + medius): the engine of run propulsion + lateral stability. Hip thrusts, single-leg bridges, lateral band walks, Bulgarian split squats.
  • Calves & posterior chain (gastrocnemius, soleus, tibialis posterior): absorb impact, store/release elastic energy. Heavy slow calf raises (both straight + bent knee), heel drops, eccentric work. Most under-trained tissue group in this sport.
  • Single-leg work across all of the above, running is a single-leg sport. Bilateral lifts build raw strength; single-leg lifts transfer.

Run drills earn 5–10 min twice a week. They wake up the neuromuscular patterns that survive when fatigue hits late in races, and they marginally lower injury risk by reinforcing good mechanics. Standard rotation: A-skip, B-skip, high knees, butt kicks, bounding, carioca, strides (6 × 20 s, full rest). Done before the run, not after.

Borderline insane summer challenges (optional, fun)

Some of you are wired for big-effort single sessions. Here are a few that I’ve done myself or watched athletes finish. None of these are required; some of them will hurt for days. All of them are great stories. If one calls to you, put it on the calendar with a friend.

Read this first

Challenges are fun and they help with drive, but they are not the main work of summer. Your real priorities are skills development (see the swim, bike, run drills above) and building a strong, durable body. Pick one challenge as a stretch goal if you want, then go back to the boring consistent training that actually compounds.

Endurance challenges

The classics. Put aside a full day and bring snacks.

Swim

The Hero set: 100×100

100 reps of 100 metres or 100 yards, on a sensible interval. Most pools do this on 1:30 or 1:45. Total: 10 km if your pool is metric, about 9.14 km if it’s a 25-yard pool. A right of passage for any serious swimmer. Plan it, fuel it, recruit a friend.

Smart version: negative-split the back half, mix in pull, paddles, and drills in the middle sets, sing the last 10.

Bike

Everesting

8,848 metres (29,029 ft) of climbing in a single ride. No sleep break. Pick a hill, climb it until you’ve summited Everest. Outside on a real climb, or indoors on Zwift / TrainerRoad / Wahoo SYSTM. Veloviewer verifies outdoor attempts.

For LA athletes: Mandeville Canyon gives roughly 315 m of climbing per rep. That works out to ~28 climbs of Mandeville to hit Everest (8,848 / 315 = 28.1). Plan 16 to 24 hours on the bike. Yes, really.

Run

The Zatopek session: 100×400

Named for Emil Zatopek, who reportedly trained on it. 100 reps of 400 m, separated either by a 200 m float (total close to 60 km of work, most of it running) or by a 90-second rest interval (total 40 km of work, mostly active recovery in between). Pick the format that matches your fitness. Use a track if you have one. Bring more gels than you think.

Smart version: first 30 reps at marathon pace, middle 40 at half-marathon pace, last 30 at marathon pace. Walk or jog the floats. Eat real food at rep 50.

Strength & control benchmarks

Less time per session, just as much character building. Each of these is a real test of strength-to-weight + control.

15 clean pull-ups

  • Strict, no kip, full hang, chin clearly over the bar
  • Builds the upper body that your swim catch needs
  • Most collegiate triathletes can’t do this on day one. Almost all can build to it
  • Way too hard right now? Start with Australian pull-ups (bar at hip height, body angled, feet on the ground), then progress to negatives, then half range, then full

35 single-leg calf raises

  • Standing on one foot, full range of motion, top and bottom
  • Each leg, 35 reps unbroken
  • Probably the single best Achilles / soleus durability test for a runner
  • If you can’t get to 25 right now, this is exactly why you might want it

Returning from a run injury? A line of research out of Stanford (and other groups) frames single-leg calf raise capacity as a useful readiness marker for getting back to running. The exact threshold varies by study, but the consistent thread is: low calf endurance + return to running = elevated re-injury risk. Build the calves first, then add the miles.

15 toes-to-bar

  • Hang from a bar, bring your toes to it, strict (no swing)
  • Crushes the deep core that holds your aero position
  • Use a partner / phone to verify your toes actually touch
Fuel for big efforts and recovery

If you’re going to attempt any of these, eat like you mean it. Endurance challenges burn a stupid number of calories; calisthenics challenges build muscle that needs protein to rebuild. Make sure your daily protein intake is high enough, especially across heavy training blocks. Rough rule for endurance athletes: 1.6 to 2.2 g of protein per kg of body weight per day, spread across 4 to 5 meals.

Strength training: the off-season superpower

The single highest-ROI thing you’re probably not doing enough of.

Cyclists, runners, triathletes all have a habit of treating strength like a chore to “get back to later.” That’s how you end up with chronic patella tendon issues at 21, a stress reaction at 22, and a back that complains every time you sit in the aero position. Summer is when you fix that.

Phase 01 · June – mid-July

Anatomical adaptation (AA)

4–6 weeks of high-rep, low-load work (12–20 reps, 2–3 sets). Goal: wake up tendons, ligaments, stabilisers, connective tissue. Movements include split squats, single-leg RDLs, push-ups, rows, Pallof presses, deadbugs.

Why: tendons adapt 3–5× slower than muscle. Skipping AA = stress injuries when intensity ramps in August.

Phase 02 · mid-July – August

Hypertrophy & strength

4–6 weeks of moderate-load work (6–10 reps, 3–4 sets). Goal: actual muscle mass + maximal strength. Movements include back squats, deadlifts, bench press, pull-ups, single-leg work, hip thrusts, weighted core.

Yes, hypertrophy is the goal. The “I’ll get bulky” fear is for people who don’t run 25+ km/week. You won’t. Trust me.

Phase 03 · September onwards

In-season maintenance

Once racing starts: 1–2 short sessions/wk (20–30 min). Heavy but low volume, mostly to retain what you built. Stop dieting yourself out of the muscle you just gained.

Sets, reps, and the rule that matters most

Form > load. Always. If your knees cave in on a squat or your back rounds on a deadlift, the strength you build is the strength to hurt yourself. Film one set from the side per session, send it in the WhatsApp group, get feedback.

UCLA Wooden Center weight room hours, check the rec sports schedule. Group sessions on Tuesdays/Thursdays once school starts.

Fueling: eat enough, race ready

RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport) is the single biggest hidden problem in collegiate endurance athletes. Let’s not be that statistic.

What RED-S is · the 30-second version

When your training energy expenditure consistently exceeds your intake, your body shuts down “optional” systems: hormones drop, immune function dips, periods stop (women), libido / morning erections vanish (men), bone density falls. You feel slow, you keep getting sick, performance plateaus and then drops. It can take years to unwind. Easier to prevent than fix.

Eat breakfast within 60 min of waking

  • Carbs + protein + a little fat
  • Skipping breakfast + training = RED-S fast lane
  • Oats + Greek yogurt + banana = 90 seconds

Refuel within 60 min after sessions

  • ~30 g carbs + ~20 g protein minimum
  • Chocolate milk, recovery shake, rice + chicken, PB&J
  • Not optional, even after “easy” days

Fuel during sessions > 75 min

  • 30–60 g carbs/hr on bike + long runs
  • Gels, Maurten, Neversecond C30, real food
  • Train your gut to tolerate intake, it’s a skill

Don’t under-eat on rest days

  • RMR keeps running. Recovery needs fuel
  • Calorie cycling is fine; calorie crashing is not
  • Rule of thumb: never below body weight (kg) × 30 kcal
⚠ Signs you might be under-fueling
  • Resting HR creeping up week-over-week
  • Constant low-grade fatigue / disturbed sleep
  • Frequent colds, slow recovery from minor injuries
  • Loss of menstrual cycle (women) / loss of morning erections, libido (men)
  • Stalled or declining performance despite more training

If three of these are happening: stop, talk to me, see a sports physician. I can test for it.

I’m brand-new to one of the three sports. Help.

Great. Summer is the time to be bad at things. Pick the weakest sport, do two short sessions/week minimum, focus 80% on technique. You’ll be embarrassingly improved by September. Promise.

I have an injury / niggle. What now?

Tell me before you train through it for three weeks and turn a niggle into surgery. I’ll modify the plan, swap sessions, get you to a PT if needed. Most niggles get fixed in a week of smart loading, if you act early.

How much sleep should I actually get?

8–9 hours is the honest answer. 7 if you’re a freak. Below 7 you’re training for resistance, not adaptation. Sleep is the single most powerful legal PED. Treat it that way.

Can I race in the summer?

Yes. A low-key Olympic or 70.3 in July or August is a great fitness check. Tell me 4 to 6 weeks out so I can plan the taper with you. Don’t race a half-IM with two weeks’ notice, or if you’re prone to foot injury. (You know who you are.)

Anything I’ll actually need to spend on?

Coaching, scheduling, the hub, summer guidance, the calculators, the 20-levers course, race-day support: all part of the roster. Race-day fuel products and any non-team races you sign up for are on you, like anywhere. Don’t feel pressure to buy gear or supplements you don’t need. If you ever want to dig deeper with lab testing or anything beyond the team setup, that’s a separate conversation, just ask me.

What if my goal is just to have fun and finish?

Perfect. The plan still applies, just at lower volume. Triathlon is for everyone; performance is a spectrum. Show up, train consistently, finish your races. That’s already 90th percentile of human beings.
