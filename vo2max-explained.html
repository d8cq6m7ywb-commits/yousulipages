The big idea

One idea, and the rest of the report follows.

VO2max is the size of your engine

Think of your aerobic system as the engine in a car. VO2max is its size: the most oxygen your body can pull in, ship to the muscles, and burn per minute when you are absolutely flat out. A bigger engine can do more work before it runs out of air. That is the whole idea.

Here is the part that matters. You almost never race at VO2max, you would last a few minutes at most. What a big engine really buys you is headroom: your cruising effort, the pace you hold for hours, sits at some percentage of that ceiling. Raise the ceiling and your comfortable pace rises with it, even though you are working at the same relative effort.

So the number is not bragging rights. It is the roof over every other pace you own. We measure the roof directly, we do not guess it from a watch.

What we actually measured

Your report has four things on it. Here is what each one is.

Metric 01

VO2max

Reported in millilitres of oxygen per kilogram of body weight per minute (ml/kg/min). The size of the engine, adjusted for how much body it has to move. This is the headline number, and the one we retest to see if the engine is growing.

Metric 02

VT1 and VT2, your two gears

Two turning points in your breathing tell us where your body changes fuel strategy. VT1 is the top of all-day easy: below it you could talk in sentences and go for hours. VT2 is the redline: the hardest effort you can hold before things unravel fast. The space between them is where most training lives.

Metric 03

Your personal zones

Because we found VT1 and VT2 on your data, the heart-rate, pace, and power zones on your report are yours, not a generic chart pulled from your age. This is the practical payoff: you leave knowing exactly what number an easy day and a hard day should feel like.

Metric 04

Fat and carb burn (fuel notes)

The mask also showed which fuel you were burning at each intensity, including your fat-max: the effort where you burn fat fastest. That feeds directly into how you fuel long days and where easy training pays off most.

Coach note

A watch or a field test estimates VO2max from heart rate and pace. That is a guess built on assumptions about you that are often wrong. The mask measures the actual gas you exchanged, breath by breath. Same word, completely different confidence.

The number that moves

Where you start matters far less than which direction you are heading. A trained athlete can add several points over a season with the right stimulus, and we retest every 8 to 12 weeks precisely so we can see the trend instead of guessing at it.

What to do with it

A test you do not act on is just a fact. Here is how this one changes your week.

Train the zones

Easy days easy, hard days hard

The most common mistake I fix is the grey middle: too hard to recover, too easy to build. Your VT1 number is the ceiling for genuinely easy work. Stay under it on easy days and you can afford to hit the hard days properly.

Raise the ceiling

A little time near the top

The engine grows from a small, well-placed dose of work up near VO2max, on top of a big base of easy volume. Not much, but it has to be there. Your report tells us the effort that counts as “up near the top” for you.

Convert it

Turn the number into pace and power

Use your VO2max with the VO2 to running, cycling power, and swimming calculators to sanity-check race paces, and set training heart-rate zones with the HR zones tool.

In practice

A club runner I test might come in at 48, put in a good winter, and retest at 52. On paper that is four points. On the road it usually shows up as the pace that used to sit at threshold now feeling a notch easier, the same effort buying a little more speed. That is what raising the ceiling actually feels like.

The science, briefly

Why I care about this number beyond race day.

Per +1 MET in VO2max
−13%

All-cause mortality risk. VO2max is one of the strongest independent predictors of longevity we have, ahead of most things your doctor measures.

Distance-run finish times
70–80%

Of the variance in finish times between well-trained distance runners is explained by VO2max alone. It is not everything, but it is a lot.

Per +1 ml/kg/min
~30 s

Off a 40 km cycling time trial, roughly. Small gains in the lab compound into real minutes across a season.

“Cardiorespiratory fitness should be considered a clinical vital sign, a stronger predictor of mortality than smoking, hypertension, or type-2 diabetes.”
American Heart Association, Scientific Statement (Ross et al., Circulation 2016)

Common misreads

Where people go wrong reading this number. Do not be one of them.

Misread 01

“Highest VO2max wins.” It does not. Two athletes with the same engine can finish minutes apart, because the winner uses less oxygen at the same pace (better economy) and holds a higher fraction of the ceiling before cracking (better threshold). VO2max sets the roof. Economy and threshold decide how much of the room you actually use. Those two are exactly what the full-stack profile measures directly, if you would rather know them than guess.

Misread 02

“My watch says a different number.” Your watch is guessing from heart rate and pace. We measured the gas. When they disagree, the mask is right. Use the watch for trends day to day, use the lab number as the anchor those trends hang from.

Misread 03

“It dropped, I’m falling apart.” A single number bounces with sleep, heat, how fresh you were, even a slightly different mask fit. That is exactly why we retest and read the trend line, not one dot. One test is a snapshot, three tests are a story.

“The best athletes in the world don’t necessarily have the highest VO2max. They have the highest economy and metabolic flexibility, that’s what separates them at the limit.”
Dr Iñigo San Millán, Performance Lead, UAE Team Emirates

Quick questions

The three I get asked most after this test.

How often should I retest?

Every 8 to 12 weeks during a block of training. Any sooner and normal day-to-day noise drowns out the real change. It is the trend across a few tests that tells the story, not any single number.

Why does my watch show a different VO2max?

It is estimating from your heart rate and pace. We measured the gas you actually exchanged. Use the watch as a daily trend line and the lab number as the fixed point it hangs from.

Can I raise it, or is it just genetics?

Both, and there is more room than most people assume. Your genetics set the range you can reach, your training decides where in that range you land. It also drifts down slowly after 40, and consistent training is what slows the drift.
