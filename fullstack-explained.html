The big idea

One idea that turns a wall of data into a plan.

A chain is only as strong as its weakest link

Every endurance effort runs down a supply chain for oxygen with three links in a row. Your lungs load oxygen into the blood. Your heart and blood deliver it to the muscles. Your muscles accept it and burn it to make power. Air in, delivered, burned. That is the whole engine, start to finish.

Here is the ruthless part: one weak link caps the entire chain, no matter how strong the other two are. World-class lungs and a huge heart do you no good if the muscle cannot use what arrives. Most athletes train all three links equally, or worse, train the strong one because it feels good. That is wasted work.

The full-stack test watches all three links at once, plus how you handle heat and fluid, so we can point at the single link that is holding you back. Fix that one, and everything downstream of it gets faster at the same time.

1

Intake

Lungs draw air and load oxygen into the blood. Rarely the limiter in healthy athletes, but we confirm it.

2

Transport

Heart and haemoglobin ship oxygen to the working muscles. Central delivery, the classic engine-size question.

3

Utilisation

Muscle fibres accept and burn the oxygen in their mitochondria. Often the quiet, trainable limiter.

What we actually measured

Several sensors, one synchronised timeline. Here is what each stream adds.

Stream 01

Breath-by-breath oxygen

Your VO2 across the whole range, including VO2max, your thresholds, your fat-max, and your glycolytic gear. The transport link, measured directly.

Stream 02

Muscle oxygen (SmO2 and tHb)

Sensors on your working muscles show whether the tissue is actually using the oxygen that arrives. This is how we see the utilisation link, the one a VO2 test alone cannot show.

Stream 03

Blood lactate

Progressive finger-sticks pin down your thresholds precisely and show how your fuel mix shifts as the effort climbs. The confirmation layer under the gas data.

Stream 04

Core temperature

A non-invasive core sensor tracks your body temperature second by second, so we can see how heat builds and whether thermoregulation is a limiter for you on hot race days.

Stream 05

Sweat and sodium loss

A wearable sweat sensor measures how much fluid and sodium you lose, and how fast. This feeds straight into a race-day hydration plan built on your numbers, not a generic bottle.

Stream 06

Economy and effectiveness

Overlaid on all of it, how much oxygen you burn at a given pace or power (your miles per gallon), and how much of your effort actually moves you forward versus bouncing or swaying.

Coach note

The magic is not any single sensor, it is that they run on one clock. Watching oxygen delivery, muscle usage, lactate, and heat rise together, second by second, is what lets us separate a delivery problem from a usage problem. Run separately, these tests each tell a partial story. Run together, they name the limiter.

Reading the map

Your report ranks your limiters. Here is the logic behind the ranking.

The limiter heat-map

Instead of one score, you get a ranked list: cardiac output, oxygen delivery, glycolytic capacity, movement economy, heat tolerance, hydration. The one at the top is where your next block of training has the most to gain. The ones at the bottom are already strengths, leave them alone for now.

Delivery versus usage, the key split

The most useful thing this test resolves: if your muscle oxygen crashes while lactate stays modest and oxygen keeps climbing, the problem is peripheral, the muscle cannot use what arrives (a utilisation limiter). If delivery itself tops out first, it is central (a transport limiter). These two need completely different training, and telling them apart is exactly what the combined sensors do.

On economy

Two athletes with the same engine can finish far apart because one uses less oxygen at the same pace. Economy is often the quiet separator at the top, and unlike VO2max it keeps improving for years. Your report benchmarks yours against norms so you know if it is a strength or a project.

In practice

Two runners come in with the same VO2max, 60. One burns noticeably less oxygen to hold marathon pace, his running economy is better, and over 42 km that is the difference between a strong negative split and walking the last 5. Economy does not show up on a VO2max test by itself. This is the test that lays it over the rest of your physiology, so we can see whether it is your strength or your next project.

The science, briefly

Why the whole picture beats a pile of separate tests.

The chain rule
1 weak link

Caps the whole system. Strengthening a link that was never your limiter buys you almost nothing, which is why finding the real one is the highest-value thing we do.

What separates the best
Economy

Not raw VO2max. At the top, economy and metabolic flexibility decide races, and both keep improving long after the engine plateaus.

Heat, the hidden limiter
Core temp

A well-trained athlete can still fall apart when core temperature climbs. Measuring it means your hot-race plan is built on data, not hope.

Common misreads

Where athletes go wrong with a big dataset.

Misread 01

“More of my strength is the answer.” It is the opposite. Doubling down on your strongest link, because it feels good and the numbers look nice, leaves your actual limiter exactly where it was. The report exists to point you at the uncomfortable link, not the flattering one.

Misread 02

“A big engine means I’m set.” A huge VO2max with a weak utilisation link still stalls, because the muscle cannot burn what the heart delivers. Engine size is one link of three. This test checks all three so you do not train the wrong one for a year.

Misread 03

“It’s a lot of data, so I’ll act on all of it.” Please do not. The whole point of ranking your limiters is to give you one thing to work on at a time. Chase all six at once and you will make progress on none. Top of the list, one block, retest.

Quick questions

The three I get asked most after a full-stack.

It is a lot of data. Where do I start?

At the top of the ranked limiter list, and nowhere else yet. One limiter, one block, then retest and move to the next. Chasing all of them at once is how you make progress on none.

How often should I run a full-stack?

Once or twice a year, at the hinges of your season. It is the deep scan. The single tests (VO2max, DARK, aero, recovery) are the quicker check-ins between them.

Do I need every sensor each time?

For a true full-stack, yes, that is the whole point: watching delivery, muscle use, lactate, heat, and sweat on one clock is what separates a delivery problem from a usage one. If you only want one slice, we run the matching single test instead.
