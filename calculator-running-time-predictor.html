How to read the results

Three columns, three different days. Same fitness underneath — different execution on top.

01

Perfect day

Cool weather, flat course, fresh legs, well-fueled, even pacing, race-rested. Best 5–10% of your race outcomes — the day everything clicks.

02

Normal day

What you should expect on most race days — reasonable conditions, decent fueling, a little wind or hills, slightly off pacing. Plan around this column.

03

Poor day

Heat, hills, headwind, cramps, stomach issues, started too fast, undertrained for the distance. Not a disaster — just the rough end of the curve.

Each cell shows the projected finish time, plus the average pace per 400 m, per km, and per mile. The 400 m split is your interval-track reference. Per-km and per-mile are your race-day watch references.

Coach note

It’s a power-law projection — not a coaching plan.

The math behind this calculator is a Riegel-style power law: T2 = T1 × (D2/D1)k. The exponent k reflects how much you slow down as the distance grows. We use 1.03 for a perfect day, 1.06 for normal, and 1.12 for poor — bracketing the 1.05–1.07 range that fits most well-trained endurance athletes.

A few things this doesn’t know:

  • Your aerobic base — a sub-3:00 marathoner’s 5K projects very differently from a sprinter’s
  • How specifically you’ve trained for the target distance (long runs, fueling practice, threshold volume)
  • Course profile, weather, altitude, your gut on the day

Use it as a sanity check and a pacing target, not a guarantee. For numbers grounded in your physiology — lactate threshold, VO2max, fat/carb crossover — come into the lab.
