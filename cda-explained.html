The big idea

One picture to hang everything else on.

Drag is a headwind you carry everywhere

At any real cycling speed, most of your effort does not go into the road or the hill. It goes into shoving air out of the way. Above roughly 30 km/h, the large majority of your watts are spent on wind alone. You are, in effect, riding into a headwind you brought with you, and its strength is set by how big and how messy a hole you punch through the air.

That hole has a number: CdA. Lower CdA, smaller hole, less air to move, more speed for the same watts. And here is why I love it: your engine takes months of training to add a few watts, but your position and kit can hand you the same speed this afternoon. CdA is the cheapest watts you will ever buy, if you can hold the position.

That last clause is the whole game. A brutally low position you can only hold for five minutes is theatre. We measure the aero you can actually keep for the full race.

What we actually measured

Your report is a list of changes and what each one bought. Here is how to read it.

Metric 01

CdA (your drag number)

Written as a small number in square metres (m²), often between 0.20 and 0.36. It is the size of your hole in the air. Everything else on the report is about pushing this number down without wrecking your power or comfort.

Metric 02

Watts saved per change

Each change we tested (helmet, hand position, hydration setup, tyre choice) shows up as a number of watts saved or lost at race speed. This is the honest scoreboard: some “aero” upgrades do nothing for your body, and now you know which.

Metric 03

Minutes saved on race day

Watts are abstract, minutes are not. We translate your drag savings into time over your actual race distance, so a change is worth making only if the clock agrees, not just the sensor.

Metric 04

Cost per minute of speed

The quiet superpower of this test. A position tweak that saves 3 minutes costs nothing. A race wheel that saves 3 minutes costs thousands. Ranking every option by dollars per minute keeps you from buying speed you could have had for free.

Coach note

A wind tunnel runs around $1,000 for two hours in a perfect, still box. We test on the road you actually race, in the wind you actually get, for a fraction of that. The number is real-world because the conditions are real.

Where you land

Your exact CdA is on your report. This is the neighbourhood it sits in.

Rough CdA bands for road and triathlon riders, in square metres. Lower is faster.

Above 0.36
Untrained or upright position. Sitting up on the hoods, no aero thought yet. The biggest, cheapest gains of your life are sitting right here.
0.30 to 0.36
Average road position. A normal fit rider on drops. Room to improve with position work before you spend a cent on kit.
0.24 to 0.30
Good TT or aero-road setup. Where most well-set-up triathletes sit. Solid, and the place fine-tuning starts to earn its keep.
0.20 to 0.24
Good time-trial position. Dialled fit, disciplined on the bars, aero kit that works. Fast.
Below 0.20
Elite time-trial territory. Rare, hard-won, and only holdable with real specificity training. Impressive if it is genuinely sustainable.
The rule of thumb

A 0.05 m² reduction in CdA is worth roughly 20 watts at 40 km/h, and each 0.01 m² off is about 4 watts. That first number beats most training blocks, which is why aero is the first place I look for free speed on the bike.

What to do with it

Free speed is only free if you actually collect it.

Position first

The best changes cost nothing

Start at the top of your ranked list, where the savings are biggest and the price is zero: lower the head, narrow the hands, tuck the shoulders. Position gains beat purchases nearly every time, and they travel with every bike you own.

Then hold it

Train the aero position

An aero position is a skill and a fitness, not just a fit. Practise holding it at race effort until it is comfortable, or it will quietly evaporate on race day when you sit up to breathe. Aero you cannot hold is not aero.

Then spend

Buy only what the clock rewards

Use your cost-per-minute ranking to decide any purchase, and sanity-check new setups with the CdA estimate calculator before you commit real money to a wheel or helmet.

Common misreads

Where riders go wrong with an aero number.

Misread 01

“Lower is always better.” Only if you can hold it and still make power. Slam the front end too far and you lose watts from a crushed hip angle and lose the position entirely when your back gives up at kilometre 40. The best CdA is the lowest one you can sustain for the whole race while pedalling strongly.

Misread 02

“The expensive wheels are the fix.” Usually not first. Your body is around 80% of the drag, the bike is the rest. Fix the engine’s housing (you) before you re-skin the frame. Buy the wheel later, if the clock still asks for it.

Misread 03

“One good test and I’m set forever.” Your position drifts as your fit, flexibility, and kit change. Retest after a bike fit, a new cockpit, or a winter of mobility work. Small drifts add up to watts you did not mean to give back.
