Your metabolic test.

What your body burns at rest, and whether you are fuelling it

The big idea

One idea that reframes the whole report.

Your body has an idle, and it dims the lights when you underfeed it

Even parked, an engine idling burns fuel just to stay running. Your body does the same: keeping your heart beating, your brain thinking, your temperature up, your cells repairing, all before you move a muscle. That baseline burn is your resting metabolic rate, and for most people it is 60 to 70% of everything they burn in a day. We measured yours directly instead of guessing it from a formula.

Here is the part that matters for your health. When you do not eat enough to cover training plus that idle, your body does not simply “lose weight.” It protects itself by turning the idle down. It dims the non-essential systems: it defers bone repair, quiets your hormones, dials back your immune system, and lowers your metabolic rate to survive on less.

A suppressed resting burn is one of the earliest visible signs of that dimming, and it shows up on this test long before a stress fracture or a lost period does. That is really why I run it: to catch under-fuelling while it is still just a number, not an injury.

What we actually measured

Four things come off this test. Here is what each one is for.

Metric 01

Your measured resting burn

Your real RMR in calories per day, from the gas you exchanged, not a formula. Generic equations can miss it by 15 to 25%, which is the difference between a fuelling plan that works and one built on a guess.

Metric 02

Measured versus predicted

We compare your real burn to what a standard formula predicts for someone your size. If your measured rate sits well below predicted, the body has turned the idle down, and that ratio is the single most useful early flag on the whole report.

Metric 03

Body composition

Your fat-free mass, the metabolically active tissue. It matters because both your resting burn and your energy availability are read per kilogram of fat-free mass, so we need it to make the rest of the numbers mean anything.

Metric 04

Your fuelling targets

Calorie and macro ranges for rest days, training days, and heavy days, built on your measured burn. Real numbers to eat to, plus example meal templates, instead of whatever an app guessed from your age and weight.

The RED-S flag

The quiet problem this test is built to catch.

When you consistently take in less energy than your training and your idle need, you drift into low energy availability (LEA), and its clinical form, Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S). It is not a fringe problem: in one study of 220 athletes across 19 sports, close to half tested positive, and the risk was equal in men and women. It is common, it is quiet, and it wrecks performance and health long before it announces itself.

What under-fuelling looks like

RED-S is the silent performance killer, and it shows up everywhere at once: stalled adaptation despite hard training, frequent colds and niggles, stress fractures, a lost or irregular period, low libido, mood crashes, poor sleep, and weight that will not move no matter what you do. If several of these ring true, this test is worth taking seriously, and worth a conversation with me and, where it fits, a sports physician or dietitian.

Coach note

This is the opposite of a diet page. Under-fuelling is not discipline and over-restriction is not a virtue in endurance sport, it is a slow leak in your health and your results. Fuelling like an athlete is not the same as dieting. The goal here is to catch the leak early and close it, not to eat less.

Where you land

Two numbers on your report tell the fuelling story. Here is how to read them.

Measured ÷ predicted RMR (the metabolic ratio)
Around 1.0 or above
Your measured burn matches or beats what your size predicts. The idle is running normally, a good sign you are fuelling the work.
0.90 to 1.0
A little below predicted. Worth watching, especially if training is climbing or the symptoms above sound familiar. Often an early nudge to eat more, not less.
Below 0.90
A strong flag. A suppressed resting burn at this level tracks with low thyroid activity and suppressed sex hormones. This is the body dimming the lights, and it is the number I most want to move back up.
Energy availability, in calories per kg of fat-free mass per day
45 and above
Optimal. Enough energy left over, after training, to run every system that keeps you healthy and adapting.
30 to 45
Sub-optimal. Function starts to compromise here, quietly. Fine for a short, deliberate window, a problem as a way of life.
Below 30
The RED-S risk zone. Below this line the body reliably starts shutting down non-essential systems. This is where the real damage accumulates.

Energy availability is intake minus exercise energy, divided by your fat-free mass. Thresholds after Loucks. Your exact figures are on your report and we will read them together.

What to do with it

Fuel the work, and use real numbers to do it.

Fuel the work

Eat to your day, not to an app

Your targets change with your training: a rest day and a five-hour day are not the same meal. Match intake to the work in front of you, and treat the occasional croissant as fuel, not a failure. Consistent under-fuelling costs you far more than the odd indulgence ever will.

Run the numbers

Set calories and macros from data

Build your intake from your measured burn with the RMR and macro tools, and estimate a session’s cost with the calorie burn calculator so the training side of the equation is honest too.

Close the leak

Recover the idle before you push

If your ratio was low, the first job is not a bigger training block, it is fuelling the idle back up. Adaptation, immunity, and bone all come back online when the energy is there. Then we build. A retest in a few months shows whether the lights came back on.

In practice

A masters runner comes in sure her slow metabolism is why the weight will not move. Her measured resting burn is 15% below what the formula predicts, a ratio of 0.86. She is not eating too much, she is eating too little, and her body has quietly turned the idle down to cope. We add food, the resting burn climbs back over the next few months, and the training finally starts sticking again. That is the flag doing its job.

Common misreads

Where people get this exactly backwards.

Misread 01

“Low RMR means a slow metabolism, so I should eat less.” Almost always the opposite. A suppressed resting burn usually means you are already under-fuelled, and eating less digs the hole deeper. The fix that raises the idle back up is more food, not less. This is the misread that does the most damage.

Misread 02

“RED-S is a women’s problem.” The risk is equal in men. Men just lose different things first: testosterone drops, bone thins, drive and mood fall off, and training stops sticking. If you are a male athlete who assumed this did not apply to you, it does.

Misread 03

“I feel fine, so I’m fuelled.” The whole reason to measure this is that the dimming happens quietly, below the level you can feel. By the time under-fuelling announces itself as a stress fracture, a missed period, or a season of colds, it is well advanced. A number catches it early. A symptom catches it late.

Quick questions

The three I get asked most after this test.

What actually changes my resting burn?

Mostly your fat-free mass, more muscle means a higher burn, and how well-fuelled you are. Chronic under-fuelling drives it down, consistent fuelling and training bring it back up. Crash diets can suppress it for years, which is worth knowing before you try one.

Will fixing a low ratio make me gain weight?

Usually not the way you fear. Recovering a suppressed resting burn often means your body finally runs its systems properly again, and many athletes lean out as training starts working. This is about fuelling health and performance, not chasing the scale in either direction.

How often should I retest?

If your ratio was low, a few months after a real fuelling reset, so we can watch the resting burn climb back. Otherwise once or twice a year is plenty to keep an eye on it.
