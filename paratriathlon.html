A plain-language guide

Paratriathlon.

Same three sports, adapted so more people can race them

Paratriathlon is swim, bike, and run for athletes with a physical or visual impairment. It is on the Paralympic programme, it has a clear set of categories so athletes compete against others with similar impairments, and it also has an open door for anyone who wants to race but does not fit, or does not want, the formal categories. This page walks through how it all works: the format, the classifications, the open division, and how a para race actually runs on the day.

What paratriathlon is

Triathlon, adapted, and fully part of the sport.

The same race, with the adaptations that let more people do it

A paratriathlon is a triathlon: a swim, then a bike, then a run, back to back, with two transitions in between. What changes is the equipment and the support that let athletes with different impairments race safely and fairly. A visually impaired athlete races with a guide. A wheelchair athlete uses a handcycle for the bike and a racing chair for the run. A standing athlete may run on a prosthetic. The clock still runs the whole way through, transitions included.

The sport is governed by World Triathlon internationally and by USA Triathlon here, and it has been on the Paralympic programme since its debut at Rio 2016. Team USA has grown fast: four medals at Rio, five at Tokyo, eight at Paris 2024, with Los Angeles hosting in 2028. Underneath the Paralympic level sits a full pathway of youth, junior, development, and age-group racing, which is where almost everyone actually starts.

The race format

The standard paratriathlon distance is a sprint.

Shorter distances for the youngest athletes

Youth and junior racing (ages 7 to 18) runs a scaled version, around a 200 m swim, 10 km bike, and 2 km run, under the open-division rules, with no classification required. It is designed to be a first race, not a qualifier. Longer para events exist too, but the sprint is the standard and the Paralympic distance.

The two divisions

The first fork: do you want to race the formal categories, or the open one?

The competitive pathway

Championship Paratriathlon

For athletes who follow the full USA Triathlon and World Triathlon competition rules and who classify into one of the official categories (the PTWC, PTS, and PTVI classes below). This is the road to national championships and, at the top, the Paralympics. The rules are stricter here because the racing is head to head against others in the same class.

The open door

ATD1, the open division

Formerly called PC Open. For athletes with a disability who do not fit one of the competition categories, or simply do not want to race under the full rule set. It allows a much broader range of equipment combinations, and classification is not required. Same distances, same course, a lower barrier to the start line.

The simple way to think about it

Championship is the classified, competitive track with a Paralympic ceiling. ATD1 is the come-as-you-are track built to get people racing. Plenty of athletes start in the open division and move across to the classified one later, and plenty stay in the open division because it fits their goals perfectly.

The classification categories

In Championship racing, athletes are grouped so they compete against similar impairments. There are three families.

PTWC, wheelchair

Race with a handcycle for the bike and a racing wheelchair for the run.

PTWC1
Most impaired. Paralysis of the lower body with loss of core strength, often with upper-body impairment as well, or multiple impairments affecting the trunk.
PTWC2
Less impaired. Core muscles and trunk control are present. Includes many double leg amputees and athletes with spina bifida.

PTS, standing and ambulatory

Race on an upright bike and run with prosthetics, braces, and/or crutches. Four classes, from most to least impaired.

PTS2
Severely impaired. Double leg amputees, above-the-knee amputees, and athletes with multiple impairments.
PTS3
Significantly impaired. Multiple limb impairments, cerebral palsy, congenital limb deficiencies.
PTS4
Moderately impaired. Below-the-knee amputees, single-arm impairments such as amputation or nerve damage.
PTS5
Mildly impaired. Milder congenital limb deficiencies and comparable impairments.

PTVI, visually impaired

Race with one guide the whole way: tethered to the guide for the swim and the run, and on a tandem piloted by the guide for the bike.

PTVI1
Completely blind, little or no sense of light or shapes. Race with blackout goggles and sunglasses to keep the class fair, and get a small head start over the other visually impaired classes.
PTVI2
Severe, partially sighted. A limited sense of shapes and light.
PTVI3
Less severe, partially sighted. For example, an athlete with Stargardt macular degeneration.
Rules change, so check the source

Classifications, distances, and category rules are set by USA Triathlon and World Triathlon and are updated over time. This page is a plain-language overview to help you understand the shape of the sport, not the official rulebook. Before you enter a race, confirm the current details at usatriathlon.org and World Triathlon.

The open door: ATD1 and the wider adaptive division

The part of the sport built so that almost nobody has to be turned away.

The open division, ATD1, mirrors the three competition families but with the rules relaxed and the equipment options widened, and no classification required to enter. It is usually split three ways:

ATD1 Wheelchair

Wheelchair

Athletes who typically use a handcycle for the bike and a racing chair for the run, with more freedom in how those are set up than the championship class allows.

ATD1 Ambulatory

Ambulatory

Athletes with an impairment to one or more limbs. They may ride an upright bike, a trike, or a handcycle, whichever gets them safely around the course.

ATD1 Visually impaired

Visually impaired

Athletes who race with a guide, tethered for the swim and run and on a tandem piloted by the guide for the bike, the same support structure as the championship class.

And beyond ATD1, the adaptive triathlon categories

USA Triathlon built a further set of adaptive categories so that people who need accommodations but do not fall under the paratriathlon classes still have a place to race:

ATD2, neuro-diverse athletes who need adaptations to compete, for example athletes with Down syndrome or autism. ATD3, physical impairments that fall outside the typical para categories. ATD4, duo racing, completing the event with a co-athlete as a team. ATTMI, for a significant but temporary mobility injury that needs accommodations to take part.

How a para race actually runs

The part spectators rarely see: the choreography that makes the day work.

At the swim exit

A colour-coded lift out of the water

A race must staff the swim exit with a team of assistants, and each athlete’s swim cap colour tells that team exactly what help to give. Red means the athlete is lifted and carried from the water. Yellow means they are supported to walk or run out. Green, orange, or white means no assistance needed. White caps mark the guides.

Personal handlers

Help in transition, within limits

Wheelchair athletes get one personal handler; standing athletes only if approved; visually impaired athletes use their guide as their handler. A handler can fit a prosthetic, lift the athlete in and out of a handcycle or chair, strip a wetsuit, and repair or rack a bike. What a handler cannot do is push: any action that propels the athlete forward can mean disqualification.

Pre-transition

Where the day chairs and legs wait

When the swim exit is far from transition, there is a pre-transition area where prosthetic legs, crutches, and day chairs are staged, each in a numbered spot matched to the athlete’s race number. Wheelchair athletes transfer into a day chair here, using a specially designed sling to come out of the water, and they get the spot closest to the swim exit.

The guide relationship

Two athletes, one race

A visually impaired athlete and their guide are tethered through the swim and the run and share a tandem on the bike, so timing, communication, and trust are as much a part of the result as fitness. The guide can also repair the bike anywhere on the course, not just in transition. It is one of the most genuine partnerships in sport.

How to get started

You do not need a classification, a fancy bike, or a Paralympic dream to begin. You need a first race.

The easiest entry

Start in the open division

Enter a local or regional race in ATD1, or as an age-grouper, with no classification needed. Many events are happy to accommodate para and adaptive athletes; the key is to call the race director ahead of time to talk through the course, the swim exit, and transition access.

The development ladder

Grow into the pathway

From there, USA Triathlon runs a development series, para-friendly races, youth and junior national championships, and national championships. Talent ID camps and development teams feed the national and Paralympic pipeline for those who want to chase it.

The support around you

Guides, handlers, and coaches

You will not do it alone. Visually impaired athletes race with a guide; many athletes lean on a handler and a coach who understand the adaptations. Building that small team early makes the first race far less daunting.

Common questions

The ones I hear most from athletes new to the sport.

Do I have to be classified to race?

No. Classification is only required for the Championship division. The open division (ATD1) and the wider adaptive categories let you race without one, which is how most people begin.

What is the difference between a guide and a handler?

A guide races the entire event alongside a visually impaired athlete, tethered for the swim and run and piloting the tandem on the bike. A handler helps in and around transition, fitting prosthetics, lifting the athlete, stripping a wetsuit, but never propelling them forward.

Can visually impaired athletes really not use any sight?

In the completely blind class (PTVI1), athletes race in blackout goggles so that residual vision cannot create an advantage, which is why that class also gets a small head start. The partially sighted classes race as they are.

What if my situation does not fit any of these?

That is exactly what the adaptive categories are for. Neuro-diverse athletes, duo teams, and even a temporary mobility injury each have a category. The whole point of the open and adaptive divisions is that there is a way in for almost everyone.
