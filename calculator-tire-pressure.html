How to choose within the range

The number shown above is a range, not a single value. Within it, pressure is a comfort ↔ speed trade-off:

  • Lower (bottom of the range) — slightly slower on glass-smooth tarmac, but noticeably more comfortable. Better grip in corners and on imperfect surfaces.
  • Higher (top of the range) — marginally faster on perfectly smooth roads. Harder ride, less grip when the road gets rough.

Stay toward the lower end of the range if any of these apply:

  • You’re prone to lower-back or saddle / butt pain on long rides
  • The ride is long (4+ hours) and you need to defend comfort late in the day
  • The road surface is mixed or poor (chip-seal, expansion strips, potholes, rough chip)
  • It’s wet, or temperatures are cool enough that the tyre carcass is stiffer

A few watts paid in rolling resistance is usually a great trade for staying fresh, in control, and on the bike at the end. On rough roads the lower pressure can even be the faster choice once vibration losses are counted.

What the model assumes

Three principles drive the maths.

01

Tire drop ≈ 15%

The Berto/Heine principle: optimal tire pressure produces about 15% vertical drop under load. Pressure scales linearly with load and inversely with width. Diameter (650b vs 700C) doesn’t matter much — measured width does.

02

Hookless rims have ETRTO caps

TSS / hookless road rims must obey: ≤ 5 bar (72.5 psi) up to ~29 mm · ≤ 4.5 bar (65 psi) for 30–34 mm · ~60 psi beyond. We enforce these and warn if the recommendation hits the cap (use a wider tire if you need higher).

03

Pump-at vs ride-at

Pump in a 20 °C garage at sea level, then ride at 2 500 m and 30 °C — the gauge changes by ~5 psi. Optional advanced inputs use ideal-gas + standard atmosphere to compute the pump gauge that lands on your target ride gauge.

Coach note

Lower than you think — and you can prove it.

Most riders run 10–15 psi too high. Modern wide tires (28+ mm) on smooth tarmac are fastest and grippier at 50–70 psi for 70–80 kg riders, not the 100+ psi old habit. The reasons:

  • Crr drops with width on real-world road. A 28 mm at 65 psi rolls faster than a 23 mm at 110 psi over chip-seal, joints, expansion strips.
  • Suspension losses dominate at high pressure: tire bounces, energy goes into heat, not forward motion. Drop pressure → smooth rolling → less wasted watts.
  • Grip in corners goes up with sensible lower pressure. Hard tires skip; supple tires bite.

Test it: run the same loop at three pressures (e.g. 70, 60, 50 psi) on the same day, same gear, same route. The fastest one wins. Don’t take our word for it — the route always tells you.

For TT and triathlon athletes, pair pressure with field aero testing — the lab measures CdA + Crr in the same protocol so you can pick a tire/pressure combo that’s actually optimal for you.

Typical targets

For a 75 kg rider + 8 kg bike. Real numbers come from the calc above.

Road · 25 mm

~60 / ~72 psi

Smooth tarmac, dry. Front lower than rear because of weight split. Drop 5 psi for chip-seal or wet.

Endurance · 32 mm

~50 / ~60 psi

Wider tire, lower pressure, better real-world speed. Default for most modern road riders on rough surfaces.

Gravel · 40 mm

~30 / ~38 psi

Tubeless almost mandatory at this width. Lower for chunky surfaces, slightly higher if you’re mostly on hardpack.

MTB · 2.3" (~58 mm)

~22 / ~25 psi

Even lower for technical descents; even higher for hardpack / XC race. Tubeless mandatory below ~30 psi.

TT / Tri · 28 mm

~58 / ~72 psi

50/50 weight split flattens the front-rear gap. CdA testing usually beats pure pressure tweaking here.
