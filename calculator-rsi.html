Reactive strength index.

Drop jump · flight time · contact time

RSI is a single number that tells you how much of your bodyweight bounces back per millisecond on the ground, a key marker of running economy, sprint capacity, and tendon health. Drop a step, hit the floor, spring up, type your two timings in.

Use the calculator ↓ How to test it

Plug in your two timings

From a drop jump (step off a 20–40 cm box). Ground contact time = how long your feet were on the floor between landing and take-off. Flight time = how long you were in the air on the rebound jump. Use a contact mat, force plate, optical system, or 240+ fps video.

Drop-jump RSI only

This calculator computes drop-jump RSI (one drop, one ground contact, one rebound). Do not enter data from repeated-hop / pogo / ground-hop protocols, those are a different formulation and produce a meaningless number here. Pogos are great training for RSI (see below); they’re just not the right measurement for this tool.

How it’s calculated

Two physics formulas, no magic.

01

Jump height from flight time

h = g · tflight² / 8 with g = 9.80665 m/s². Assumes take-off and landing happen at the same height. Standard projectile motion.

02

RSI from height & contact

RSI = h / tcontact. Height per second on the ground. The lower the contact time and the higher the bounce, the better.

03

Why this number matters

RSI tracks closely with running economy, sprint mechanics, and tendon stiffness. It’s the cheapest single measurement that previews how well an athlete will respond to plyometric training.

How to test it well

Bad protocol = noise. Five details that make or break the number.

Box

20–40 cm, consistent

Same box every test. Higher boxes don’t mean higher RSI, for many athletes, the optimum is 20–30 cm. Test at the box where you produce the best score, then standardize.

Take-off

Step off, don’t jump

Step off the box with a single leg without losing height. Land stiff, spring up immediately, "as fast and as high as possible."

Arms

Hands on hips

Or the same arm action every rep. Arm swing adds height and noise, lock it down to test the spring system, not the swing system.

Reps

3–5 trials, take the best

Use the best valid rep (or mean of best 2). Discard reps where contact time blows out or technique collapses (knees caving, hips folding).

Tool

Contact mat > force plate > video

Contact mat or force plate is the gold standard. Optical systems work. Video is fine if you can shoot at 240+ fps, below that the timing error eats your signal.

How to improve it

RSI rises when contact time drops without losing jump height.

Three angles of attack, in priority order for most endurance athletes:

  • Fast-SSC plyometrics, pogos, ankle hops, low-amplitude bounds, drop/depth jumps with contact < 0.25 s. The point isn’t height, it’s short ground time. (Pogos are a great training tool here, even though they’re not the right format for measuring RSI, use the drop jump above for that.)
  • Eccentric rate-of-force, heavy strength + fast eccentrics, isometric calf and quad work, sprint drills. Builds the spring before you ask it to bounce.
  • Manage fatigue and tendon load, 2–3 sessions/week, low contact volume to start, quality over volume. Plyometrics on top of fatigue is how Achilles tendons go bad.

Track RSI every 4–6 weeks, same protocol, same box, same shoes, same time of day. Trends matter; one number doesn’t.

What your number means

The bands below come from sport-science literature (Flanagan & Comyns), valid for general athletic populations. Sprinters and jumpers will sit higher than endurance athletes at the same training level.

< 1.6

Low

Reactive strength under-developed. Not yet ready for moderate-intensity plyos, build with strength, eccentric work, and very short fast-SSC drills first.

1.6 – 2.0

Moderate

Ready for moderate-intensity plyometrics. Solid base for most age-groupers.

2.0 – 2.5

Good

Ready for high-intensity plyometrics, bounds, depth jumps, weighted jumps. Most well-trained endurance athletes top out here.

2.5 – 3.0

Excellent

Diminishing returns from more plyo volume. Time to specialize the stimulus toward the demand, sprint, jump, fast running, depending on the sport.

≥ 3.0

World-class

Elite jumper / sprinter territory. Marginal gains only. Maintenance + sport-specific transfer becomes the focus.

Coach note

RSI, GCT, and leg-spring stiffness aren’t the same thing.

Three related-but-distinct concepts that get blurred in conversation:

  • RSI rises when jump height goes up or contact time goes down. It’s output / time-on-ground.
  • Leg-spring stiffness (LSS) in spring-mass terms: stiffer legs need shorter contact times for a given height. RSI correlates with LSS but isn’t identical, LSS also depends on vertical displacement and force profile.
  • GCT alone is not RSI. Trimming GCT helps RSI only if you don’t lose jump height in the process.

Things that move RSI even at the same fitness: acute fatigue, soreness, warm-up quality, time of day, caffeine, surface and footwear stiffness, box height, athlete intent (min-GCT vs max-height instruction), and technique (hip/knee collapse blows out GCT and tanks the score).
