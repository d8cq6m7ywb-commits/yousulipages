Your results, explained

The DARK test.

Three minutes, blindfolded, everything you had

Blindfold on. No clock. Go from the gun and do not stop until I tell you to, while a mask read your oxygen and a sensor watched your muscle drain. It is loud, it is brutal, and it is over before your mind can talk you into pacing. In one 3-minute effort we pulled three numbers that usually take three separate tests. This page explains what you emptied yourself out for.

The big idea

One idea, and this is the one I use most.

A sustainable engine, and a reserve you spend on top of it

Your body makes power two ways at once. There is a sustainable system, your aerobic engine, that can run for hours as long as fuel and air keep coming. And there is a finite reserve stacked on top of it, the anaerobic tank you draw on for anything faster than the engine alone can hold. The reserve is huge while it lasts, drains fast, and has to recharge in the easy moments before you can spend it again.

Every race is a negotiation between the two. Cruise, and the aerobic engine carries you. Attack, bridge, sprint, and you spend the reserve for power the engine could never make on its own. Drain the reserve to empty and you are left with exactly what the engine can hold, no more.

The DARK test makes you floor both at once, all the way to the bottom. When the reserve is finally gone, whatever power you are still turning out is your aerobic engine alone: your critical power. And all the extra work you poured out above that line before it drained is the size of your reserve: your W′. Two numbers that decide how you race, measured by emptying you completely.

Two ways to make power, measured in one go: the sustainable oxidative system, and the fuel-and-turbo reserve you burn above it.

The sustainable engine

Critical power (aerobic)

The output you can hold more or less all day, as long as you keep fuelling. Steady, reliable, trainable over months. This is the floor you settle onto once the reserve is gone.

The finite reserve

W′ (anaerobic capacity)

A finite tank of work you can spend above critical power for surges and sprints. Huge while it lasts, empties fast, and needs easy moments to recharge. Measured in kilojoules.

Why we blindfold you

The blindfold is not theatre. It is what makes the numbers honest.

The whole trick

This test only works if you genuinely empty the reserve. The moment you can see a clock, your brain starts rationing: it holds a little back for a finishing kick, it paces to the finish line it can see coming. That saved work pollutes the result, the sustainable number comes out too high and the reserve looks smaller than it is. So we take the clock away. Blindfolded, with no idea when “stop” is coming, you cannot pace what you cannot see. You just go, and keep going, until I end it.

That is also why it is loud. The cheering, the yelling, the refusal to let you coast, all of it exists to keep you above what your mind would settle for on its own. Your job is simple and awful: give everything, right to the last second, and trust that stop will come. When you do that, the end of the effort is a true floor and the work above it is a true tank. Pace it, and the whole thing is worthless.

The payoff

Because the effort is genuinely maximal from the gun, your oxygen use very likely reached its ceiling too. So a well-run DARK test also gives us a strong read on your VO2max, three numbers from one three-minute effort.

What we actually measured

Three headline numbers, plus a live look at the reserve draining.

Metric 01

Critical power (the engine)

The power you were still holding at the end, once the reserve was empty. Your sustainable ceiling: the highest output your aerobic engine can defend for a long time. Raise this and every long effort gets faster.

Metric 02

W′ (the reserve)

All the work you produced above critical power before you drained, in kilojoules. The size of your anaerobic tank, the currency you spend on every attack, bridge, and sprint.

Metric 03

VO2max (the air)

Because the effort was all-out, your oxygen use very likely topped out, giving us a strong estimate of your aerobic ceiling in the same session.

Metric 04

Muscle drain (SmO2)

The sensor watched the oxygen crash out of your working muscle as the reserve emptied, and how it rebounded after. A live picture of the tank draining and starting to refill.

Why one test, not three

Normally the engine, the reserve, and the air each want their own protocol on their own day. The DARK test is designed so a single maximal 3-minute effort exposes all three at once. It is short, it is horrible, and it is efficient.

Where you land

Your exact numbers are on your report. Here is how to read the reserve size.

Rough W′ ranges (your anaerobic reserve, in kilojoules). Two riders can share the same critical power and race like completely different athletes because of this number.

Around 15 kJ, or less
A diesel, a steady-state engine. Small reserve, big sustainable output. You win by grinding everyone down at a hard, even pace, and you should avoid races that come down to repeated sprints.
Around 20 kJ
A balanced engine. Enough reserve to cover surges, enough diesel to hold a hard pace. Flexible, but rarely the very best at either extreme.
25 kJ and up
A sprinter or puncheur. Big reserve. You can go with every attack and win a kick, but you crack if you burn it too early. Your race is patience, then one decisive dump of the tank.
Same engine, different race

This is the whole reason the test measures both numbers. Critical power alone tells you how strong the engine is. Pair it with W′ and you know how you win: grind, or wait and pounce. For most athletes critical power sits within about 5% of the FTP you may already know, but W′ is the part your bike computer never told you.

What to do with it

Race to your reserve, and train the number you want to change.

Race your profile

Grind or pounce, on purpose

A small reserve says make it a hard, honest drag from a long way out, so the sprinters never get to use theirs. A big reserve says sit in, save it, and spend it once. Racing against your own physiology beats racing against your ego.

Grow the engine

Lift critical power for the long stuff

If your races are long and steady, the engine is your lever. Threshold and hard aerobic work raise critical power, and every sustained effort you own rises with it. The reserve matters less the longer the race.

Model it

Watch the reserve spend and refill

Your critical power and W′ are the two numbers behind your whole power-duration curve. The critical power calculator shows how the reserve drains above the line and recharges below it, which is exactly how to plan an attack that does not leave you cracked.

In practice

Two riders both hold 250 watts of critical power. One has a 15 kJ reserve, the other 25. Put them in a hilly road race and the 25 covers every attack and takes the sprint. Put them in a flat time trial and the gap all but vanishes, the reserve barely gets touched. Same engine, two completely different race plans, and this is the test that tells them apart.

Common misreads

Where athletes go wrong with this one.

Misread 01

“My peak power is the important number.” It is not. Anyone can post a big number for a few seconds. The number that decides your racing is what you were still holding when the reserve was dead, your critical power, plus how big the reserve was on the way there. Peak is a footnote.

Misread 02

“I should have paced it better.” No. Pacing is the one thing that ruins this test. A negative-split, saved-a-bit-for-the-end effort produces a critical power that is too high and a W′ that is too small, junk numbers that will mislead your whole plan. The blindfold exists precisely so you cannot do the thing you are trained to do in every race. Going out too hard is correct here.

Misread 03

“A big reserve means I’m fitter.” It means you race differently, not better. A giant W′ on a small engine gets shelled out the back of a hard, steady race before the sprint ever happens. A small W′ on a big engine wins by making it honest. Fitter is the whole picture, engine and reserve together, matched to the race you are actually doing.

Quick questions

The three I get asked most after this one.

Do I really have to do it blindfolded?

Yes, that is the point. If you can see a clock you pace, and pacing quietly ruins the numbers. Blindfolded and with no idea when it ends, you cannot save anything, which is the only way the result comes out honest.

Which matters more, critical power or W′?

It depends on your racing. The longer and steadier the race, the more your critical power decides it. The more it comes down to surges and a sprint, the more your W′ does. Most athletes want both climbing, but knowing which one your races reward tells you what to train first.

How is this different from the recovery test?

This one measures the size of your reserve and the ceiling it sits on. The recovery profile measures how fast you refill that reserve between efforts. Together they describe how much you can spend and how quickly you get it back.
