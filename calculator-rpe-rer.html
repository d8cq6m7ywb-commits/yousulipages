What RER actually tells you

And what it doesn’t.

01

Substrate ratio at steady state

RER ≈ 0.70 = predominantly fat. RER ≈ 1.00 = predominantly carb. 0.85 sits roughly 50/50. The math is the volume of CO2 you exhale divided by O2 you consume, fat oxidation produces less CO2 per O2, carb produces equal.

02

Doesn’t see protein, ketones, or PCr

Protein contributes a small, variable amount RER can’t isolate. Ketone oxidation has an effective RQ near fat, can’t separate them. Phosphocreatine powers the first ~3–10 s of all-out efforts and uses no oxygen, so it’s invisible to RER.

03

Above ~1.0, it lies

At very high intensity, lactate is buffered and exhales as extra CO2. RER can climb past 1.0 even with no actual carb-only oxidation, substrate inference becomes unreliable. Reading is most useful at RPE 4–8 / RER 0.85–0.95 when you’re in steady-state aerobic work.

Fuel systems by intensity domain

The map is rough, but the order is right.

RER ~0.75–0.82

Very easy / Z1 recovery

Predominantly oxidative. Fat > carb. Aerobic base work, recovery sessions, all-day Zone 1. Long-term metabolic adaptations live here.

RER ~0.82–0.90

Aerobic / tempo · Z2–Z3

Oxidative. Mix of fat + carb. The endurance sweet spot. Where most race-pace effort happens for events of 90 min+. Fat ox peak typically lives in here.

RER ~0.90–1.00

Threshold / heavy-severe · Z4

Oxidative + glycolytic. Carb > fat. Sustainable for 30–60 min. Where lactate threshold sits; fueling decisions matter most because carb burn rate is high.

RER > 1.00

VO2max + sprints · Z5

PCr → glycolytic dominant. RER becomes meaningless due to lactate buffering. Don’t infer substrate split here, fueling at this intensity is a different conversation.

Coach note

Real RER vs intensity is not linear, and not the same for any two athletes.

This calculator’s linear RPE→RER mapping is a teaching tool. In reality, two athletes at the same RPE can have very different RER values:

  • Trained endurance athletes burn more fat at a given intensity, their crossover (the RER point where carb takes over from fat) shifts right, sometimes by 20+ % VO2max.
  • Glycogen status matters: same effort fasted vs. carb-loaded → different RER.
  • Heat, altitude, modality all shift it. Cycling RER ≠ running RER at the same effort.
  • Diet: a fat-adapted (low-carb) athlete oxidizes fat at higher intensities, peak fat ox can hit 1.5×  that of a high-carb-fed athlete.

If you actually need your crossover and peak-fat-oxidation rate, for race-day fueling planning, weight goals, or zone setup, metabolic profiling at the lab measures it directly. The number you walk out with is the difference between guessing your fuel needs and knowing them.

Protein, gluconeogenesis & intra-workout fueling

Why fueling matters even when RER says you’re “mostly burning fat.”

Catabolism

Protein gets eaten too

Long, under-fueled efforts pull amino acids from muscle. The liver (and kidneys) convert them, plus lactate (Cori cycle) and glycerol, into glucose via gluconeogenesis. Muscle itself doesn’t export glucose; it supplies substrates (e.g. alanine via the glucose-alanine cycle).

Cost

Lean mass & power loss

Chronic under-fueling raises reliance on amino acids. Net: lean mass and power both decline. Athletes feel this as “legs heavy for no reason” weeks before the scale moves.

Practical

Fuel the work

Appropriate intra-workout carbohydrate + adequate daily protein protect performance and muscle. RER says “mostly fat” at low intensity, but that doesn’t mean fasted long sessions are free.
