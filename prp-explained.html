Your recovery profile.

How hard you can surge, and how fast you bounce back

You went all-out, recovered, and did it again, several times, while I tracked your oxygen, your muscle fuel, and your blood lactate through every surge and every recovery. Sprint, recover, repeat is the coin of the realm in crits, HYROX, CrossFit, and short-course triathlon. This page explains what your recovery numbers mean and, more importantly, which one to train.

The big idea

One idea behind the whole recovery curve.

The race is won by whoever refills fastest

Picture a matchbook. Every hard surge, the break, the last lap, the sprint out of the corner, strikes a match. You only get so many, and the box refills slowly in the easy moments between efforts. Most athletes obsess over how big their single match burns. The winner is usually the one who restocks the box fastest.

That is what this test measures. Not just how hard you can go once, but how quickly you are ready to go hard again. In a race full of surges, the athlete who recovers 5% faster between them is the one still there when it matters, while everyone else is emptying a box that never refilled.

Recovery, it turns out, is not toughness. It is a trainable, mostly aerobic skill. And the first step to training it is knowing which part of the refill is slow for you.

What we actually measured

Three data streams, tracked through every surge and every recovery.

Metric 01

Surge power, and its fade

How hard you can go on each all-out effort, and how much that peak drops across repeats. A flat line across efforts is a deep box. A steep fade means the box empties faster than it refills.

Metric 02

Time-to-recover

How many seconds it takes you to climb back to 50% and then 90% of your baseline between efforts. This is the refill rate itself, the single most race-relevant number on the report.

Metric 03

Your limiter, named

By watching oxygen, muscle oxygenation (SmO2), and lactate together, we can see why your refill is slow: whether the bottleneck is aerobic refill, buffering capacity, or blood flow into the working muscle. That diagnosis is the whole point.

Coach note

Feel is a terrible recovery gauge. Under fatigue you feel wrecked long after your body is physiologically ready, or the reverse, you feel fine while your muscle oxygen is still in a hole. The sensors do not flatter you and they do not panic. The curve is the truth.

Your limiter, and what it means

Recovery is individual, so instead of one band, your report points at one of three culprits. Here is what each one asks you to train.

Aerobic refill
Your oxygen system is slow to restock the box between efforts. The fix is aerobic base and better VO2 kinetics: more easy volume and well-placed threshold work, not more sprinting.
Buffering capacity
You make a lot of lactate and struggle to clear and buffer it, so the acid piles up across repeats. The fix leans toward controlled glycolytic work and tempo floats that train clearance.
Local muscle perfusion
The central engine is fine, but blood flow and oxygen into the specific working muscle lag. The fix is more local: targeted intervals, position and cadence work, sometimes strength for the tissue itself.
Why this matters more than a single score

Two athletes can have identical recovery times for completely different reasons, and the training that fixes one would waste the other’s season. Knowing the culprit is what turns “do more intervals” into the right intervals.

What to do with it

Train the limiter you have, not the workout you like.

Match the work

Pick intervals for your culprit

Your report ties your limiter to a session type: aerobic refill wants base and clearance work, buffering wants controlled glycolytic sets, perfusion wants targeted local intervals. Same hour of training, very different result, depending on which you choose.

Recover on purpose

Use active recovery between efforts

Lactate clears fastest at an easy-but-not-too-easy float, not at a dead stop. In races and in intervals, keeping the legs lightly turning between surges can refill the box faster than fully coasting.

Connect it

Tie it to your power profile

Your recovery lives on top of your critical power and your anaerobic reserve. The critical power tool shows the reservoir you are spending and refilling, and the DARK test is where we measure that reserve and the ceiling it sits on directly.

In practice

Two crit racers turn up with the same threshold. One is back to 90% of baseline forty seconds after a hard surge, the other needs sixty. Twenty laps in, the first has thrown ten accelerations the second could not answer, and none of it showed up in their FTP. That gap is the race, and it is trainable. That is exactly what this test goes looking for.

The science, briefly

Why recovery, not peak power, is often the hidden race-winner.

Gap in recovery speed
5% = 1st vs 5th

When the final surge hits, a small edge in how fast you refilled between the earlier ones decides who is still in contention and who is drifting back.

Repeat-sprint ability tracks
PCr, not grit

How well you hold power across repeats follows phosphocreatine resynthesis, an aerobic process, far more closely than it follows willpower.

First 2 minutes after all-out
Diagnostic

The shape of your lactate drop and SmO2 rebound in the first two minutes tells us, on its own, whether you are ready for dense interval blocks.

The readiness signal

A quick reoxygenation of the muscle after an effort is one of the most reliable “ready to go again” signals we have. A sluggish rebound says the fuel tank or the plumbing needs work first, before you pile on more intensity.

Common misreads

Where athletes go wrong with a recovery number.

Misread 01

“Recovery is just being tough.” It is not a character trait, it is physiology, and mostly aerobic physiology at that. You cannot grit your way to a faster refill. You train it, the same way you train anything else, once you know which part is slow.

Misread 02

“Bigger peak power is all that matters.” A huge single effort you cannot repeat loses to a slightly smaller one you can throw down five times. In surge-recovery racing, the size of your matchbook and the speed it refills usually beat the size of one match.

Misread 03

“I’ll just do more intervals.” More of the wrong interval trains the wrong system and digs a fatigue hole for nothing. The whole reason we named your limiter is so the next block is aimed, not just hard.

Quick questions

The three I get asked most after this test.

Is recovery really trainable?

Yes, and mostly through aerobic work, which surprises people. A better aerobic base and the right intervals for your limiter speed up how fast you refill between efforts. It is not grit, it is physiology you can build.

Which limiter is the best one to have?

None of them, they are just different jobs. The point is not to have a good one, it is to know which one is slowest for you so the next block aims at it instead of guessing.

How is this different from the DARK test?

The DARK test measures the size of your reserve and the ceiling it sits on. This one measures how fast you refill that reserve between efforts. Two sides of the same coin: how much you can spend, and how quickly you get it back.
